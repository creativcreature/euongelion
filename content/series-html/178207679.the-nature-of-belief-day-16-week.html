What is the purpose of trials, and how can we consider them “pure joy” when they feel so painful?

Today we begin our deep dive into James 1:2-8 by examining one of the most counterintuitive commands in Scripture: to consider trials “pure joy.” James doesn’t say that trials feel joyful, but that we can consider them joyful because of what they produce in us. This passage challenges us to reframe our perspective on suffering and to see it not as a meaningless interruption but as a tool God uses to develop mature, unwavering faith.

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Elias stared at the letter, the official seal of the provincial governor mocking him from the parchment. The decree was simple and brutal: all citizens were required to offer a pinch of incense to the genius of the Emperor and receive a libellus—a certificate proving their loyalty. Those who refused would have their property confiscated and face imprisonment.

For Elias, a respected merchant and a leader in the small Christian community in his city, this was not a simple political gesture. It was an act of worship, a declaration of ultimate allegiance. To offer the incense would be to deny Christ. To refuse would be to lose everything he had worked his entire life to build, and to endanger his family.

The trial was not a distant theological concept; it was a stark choice with devastating consequences. That night, the Christian community gathered secretly in his home. The fear was palpable. “What are we to do?” a young woman whispered, her voice trembling. “I have children. How can I let them starve?”

An older man, a weaver named Matthias, spoke up. “The Lord has brought us to a place of testing. This is a fire. It will show what we are made of.”

Elias felt the weight of their fear, and his own. He had no easy answers. He thought of the words of James, a letter they had studied together many times: “Consider it pure joy... whenever you face trials of many kinds.” Joy? The word felt alien, almost insulting. There was no joy in this choice. There was only loss.

Later, alone in his study, he wrestled with the passage. He couldn’t feel joy. But could he consider it joy? Could he, as an act of will, choose to see this not as a catastrophe, but as a crucible? A furnace designed to burn away the impurities in his faith—his reliance on his wealth, his reputation, his comfortable life—and leave behind something pure, something genuine?

The thought was terrifying, but it also held a strange power. It meant that this suffering was not random. It had a purpose. God was not absent; he was at work, forging something in them that ease and prosperity never could. He was producing perseverance, a steadfastness that could only be built under a heavy load.

Elias did not know what the future held. But as he prayed, he made a choice. He would not pretend to be happy. But he would lead his mind to a different conclusion. He would choose to believe that this trial, as terrible as it was, was an opportunity for God to make him and his community “mature and complete, not lacking anything.” He would choose to see it as a severe mercy.

Takeaway: The command to “consider it pure joy” is not about denying the pain of trials, but about reframing our perspective to see them as opportunities for God to produce perseverance and maturity in us.

Deep Dive (15 Minutes)

Practical and Theological Focus:
The Purpose of Trials in the Christian Life

Full Passage: James 1:2-8 (NIV)
2 Consider it pure joy, my brothers and sisters, whenever you face trials of many kinds, 3 because you know that the testing of your faith produces perseverance. 4 Let perseverance finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything. 5 If any of you lacks wisdom, you should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to you. 6 But when you ask, you must believe and not doubt, because the one who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. 7 That person should not expect to receive anything from the Lord. 8 Such a person is double-minded and unstable in all they do.

Who is James?

Before we delve into his words, we should ask: who is the author of this letter, and why should we listen to him? The author identifies himself simply as “James, a servant of God and of the Lord Jesus Christ.” Early church tradition and most modern scholars identify him not as the apostle James (who was martyred too early to have written this letter), but as James, the brother of Jesus.

This is significant for several reasons:

Initial Skepticism: The Gospels tell us that during Jesus’s earthly ministry, his own brothers, including James, were not believers (John 7:5). They thought he was out of his mind. James was not a follower from the beginning; he was a skeptic.

Eyewitness to the Resurrection: Something dramatic changed James’s mind. The apostle Paul tells us that the resurrected Jesus made a special appearance to James (1 Corinthians 15:7). This encounter transformed James from a skeptical brother into a devoted follower.

Leader of the Jerusalem Church: James became a key leader, perhaps the primary leader, of the church in Jerusalem (Acts 15, Galatians 2:9). He was known for his piety, his wisdom, and his commitment to practical righteousness. He was often called “James the Just.”

When James writes about faith, he is not writing as someone for whom belief came easily. He is writing as a former skeptic who was convinced by the overwhelming evidence of the resurrection. And when he writes about perseverance in trials, he is writing as the leader of a community that was facing intense persecution. His words carry the weight of both personal experience and pastoral authority.

The Nature of Trials

James uses the word πειρασμός (peirasmos) for “trials,” which can mean either a test of faith or a temptation to sin. In this context, James is referring to external difficulties that test and prove the genuineness of our faith. These can be “of many kinds”—financial hardship, relational conflict, persecution, illness, or any other form of suffering.

The Command to “Consider”

The Greek word for “consider” is ἡγέομαι (hēgeomai), which means “to lead the way,” “to consider,” or “to count.” It’s a leadership word, implying a deliberate, cognitive choice to lead your mind to a certain conclusion. James is not commanding an emotional response (feeling joyful) but a mental one (counting it as joy). This is a radical reframing of suffering.

The Reason for Joy

The reason we can consider trials joy is not because of the trials themselves, but because of what they produce. James says, “because you know that the testing of your faith produces perseverance.”

Testing (δοκίμιον, dokimion): This refers to the process of proving the genuineness of something. In the ancient world, it was used for testing metals to see if they were pure. Trials are God’s way of revealing the quality of our faith.

Produces (κατεργάζομαι, katergazomai): This means “to work out” or “to accomplish.” The testing of our faith isn’t pointless; it has a specific, productive outcome.

Perseverance (ὑπομονή, hypomonē): This is not passive resignation but active, steadfast endurance. It’s the ability to remain under a heavy load without collapsing. Trials build our spiritual stamina.

The Goal of Perseverance

Perseverance has its own work to do: “Let perseverance finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything.”

Mature (τέλειος, teleios): This means “perfect,” “complete,” or “fully grown.” It’s the same word used to describe a mature adult as opposed to a child.

Complete (ὁλόκληρος, holoklēros): This means “whole” or “entire,” with all its parts intact.

Not Lacking Anything (μηδενὶ λειπόμενοι, mēdeni leipomenoi): This emphasizes the all-encompassing nature of the maturity God wants to produce in us.

The ultimate goal of trials is not just to make us tougher, but to make us more like Christ—mature, complete, and lacking nothing in our spiritual character.

Old Testament Connection

This concept of God using suffering to purify and strengthen his people is woven throughout the Old Testament.

Proverbs 17:3: “The crucible for silver and the furnace for gold, but the Lord tests the heart.”

Isaiah 48:10: “See, I have refined you, though not as silver; I have tested you in the furnace of affliction.”

Zechariah 13:9: “This third I will put into the fire; I will refine them like silver and test them like gold. They will call on my name and I will answer them; I will say, ‘They are my people,’ and they will say, ‘The Lord is our God.’”

James is drawing on this rich biblical tradition to help his readers understand that their suffering is not random or meaningless, but is part of God’s purposeful plan to refine their faith.

  2. In what areas of your character do you feel immature or incomplete? How might your current trials be the very tool God is using to bring you to maturity in those areas?

Greek Language Learning: Week 7, Day 1

The Language of Trials and Maturity

Today we begin our seventh week of Greek by learning the vocabulary James uses to describe the purpose of trials.

Trials and Testing:
•πειρασμός (peirasmos) - “trial,” “test,” “temptation”
•δοκίμιον (dokimion) - “testing,” “proving” (the means of the test)
•δοκιμή (dokimē) - “proof,” “approved character” (the result of the test)
•δόκιμος (dokimos) - “tested,” “approved”

Joy and Consideration:
•χαρά (chara) - “joy,” “delight”
•ἡγέομαι (hēgeomai) - “I consider,” “I count,” “I lead”

Faith and Perseverance:
•πίστις (pistis) - “faith,” “trust”
•ὑπομονή (hypomonē) - “perseverance,” “steadfastness,” “endurance”

Maturity and Completion:
•τέλειος (teleios) - “mature,” “perfect,” “complete”
•ὁλόκληρος (holoklēros) - “whole,” “entire,” “complete in all its parts”
•λείπω (leipō) - “I lack,” “I am wanting”

Reading Practice

πᾶσαν χαρὰν ἡγήσασθε, ἀδελφοί μου, ὅταν πειρασμοῖς περιπέσητε ποικίλοις
(pasan charan hēgēsasthe, adelphoi mou, hotan peirasmois peripesēte poikilois)
“Consider it all joy, my brothers, whenever you fall into various trials”

Grammar Note: The Aorist Imperative

ἡγήσασθε (hēgēsasthe) is an aorist imperative. This tense is used for a command to do a specific action. It’s a call for a decisive choice: “Make the choice to consider it joy!”

Memory Tip

Think of ὑπομονή (hypomonē) as “remaining under” (hypo = under, monē = to remain). It’s the ability to stay strong under a heavy load.
