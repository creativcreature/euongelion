How would first-century Jews have understood Jesus’s call to “take up your cross” in the context of Roman occupation?

Today we’re stepping into the sandals of Matthew’s original readers to understand what Jesus’s call to discipleship would have meant to them. His words about taking up a cross weren’t abstract spiritual metaphors—they were concrete references to the brutal realities of life under Roman rule, and they carried political implications that could be literally life-threatening.

Day 3: What’s the Lesson for the Original Audience?

“Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me.” - Matthew 16:24

Quickstart (1 Minute Commitment)

Today’s Verse: Matthew 16:24 - “Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me.”

Greek Word: σταυρός (Stauros) - “Cross.” For first-century readers, this wasn’t a religious symbol but an instrument of Roman execution—a tool of terror used to maintain political control through public humiliation and death.

Reflection Question: What would it have meant for Jesus’s first followers to publicly identify with a symbol of Roman execution and political rebellion?

Urgency Moment: Today, do something that costs you reputation points but aligns with your deepest convictions. Speak up in a meeting. Decline a lucrative but ethically questionable opportunity. Choose integrity over image.

Prayer: “Help me understand the courage it took for the first Christians to follow Jesus in a world where discipleship could literally lead to death.”

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Dr. Michael Rodriguez sat across from his mentor, Dr. Elena Vasquez, in her office at the medical school where he had trained. Dr. Vasquez had been the first Latina department head at the university and had faced her own difficult choices throughout her career. Michael had come to her because he trusted her judgment and knew she would give him honest counsel about the decision he was facing.

“Michael, let me tell you about a choice I made thirty years ago,” Dr. Vasquez said after listening to his dilemma. “I was offered a position at a prestigious private practice that would have made me wealthy and famous. Instead, I chose to stay here and focus on training the next generation of doctors, particularly those from underserved communities. My colleagues thought I was crazy to turn down that opportunity.”

She paused, looking out the window at the medical students walking across campus. “But here’s what I learned: the path that looks like sacrifice from the outside often turns out to be the path to the deepest fulfillment. I’ve trained over 200 doctors who are now serving communities that desperately needed them. My former classmates who took the lucrative positions are successful, but many of them tell me they feel empty, like they’re just going through the motions.”

Dr. Vasquez turned back to Michael. “The question isn’t whether you’ll make sacrifices—everyone makes sacrifices. The question is what you’ll sacrifice for. Will you sacrifice the possibility of meaningful impact for the security of conventional success, or will you sacrifice conventional success for the possibility of meaningful impact?”

As Michael walked back to his car, he realized that Dr. Vasquez had helped him see his choice in a new light. It wasn’t really about Guatemala versus Johns Hopkins—it was about what kind of person he wanted to become and what kind of legacy he wanted to leave.

Takeaway: The most important choices often aren’t between sacrifice and comfort, but between different kinds of sacrifice and different definitions of success.

Deep Dive (15 Minutes)

Historical and Cultural Focus:
Discipleship in the Shadow of the Cross

Full Passage: Matthew 16:21-28 (NIV)
21 From that time on Jesus began to explain to his disciples that he must go to Jerusalem and suffer many things at the hands of the elders, the chief priests and the teachers of the law, and that he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life.22 Peter took him aside and began to rebuke him. “Never, Lord!” he said. “This shall never happen to you!”23 Jesus turned and said to Peter, “Get behind me, Satan! You are a stumbling block to me; you do not have in mind the concerns of God, but merely human concerns.”24 Then Jesus said to his disciples, “Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me. 25 For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will find it. 26 What good will it be for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul? Or what can anyone give in exchange for their soul?”

The Political Reality of Crucifixion

When Jesus spoke of taking up a cross, his audience would have immediately understood the political implications. Crucifixion was Rome’s preferred method of execution for rebels, slaves, and those who challenged imperial authority. It was designed not just to kill but to terrorize—victims were crucified along major roads as a warning to others who might consider defying Roman rule.

The cross was a symbol of Rome’s absolute power and the fate that awaited anyone who dared to challenge the empire. For Jesus to use this imagery in calling people to follow him was to explicitly identify his movement with political resistance to Roman authority.

The Jewish Context of Messianic Expectation

Matthew’s original readers were primarily Jewish Christians who had grown up with expectations of a Messiah who would overthrow Roman rule and restore Israel’s political independence. Many Jews of the first century believed that when the Messiah came, he would lead a successful military campaign against Rome and establish a new Jewish kingdom.

Jesus’s teaching about taking up the cross directly contradicted these expectations. Instead of promising victory over Rome through military might, he was calling his followers to be willing to die at Roman hands. This wasn’t the kind of messiahship most Jews were expecting or wanting.

The Challenge to Zealot Ideology

The Zealots were a Jewish political movement that advocated violent resistance to Roman rule. They believed that faithful Jews should be willing to die fighting against the occupying forces, and they viewed collaboration with Rome as betrayal of their faith and nation.

Jesus’s call to take up the cross both echoed and challenged Zealot ideology. Like the Zealots, he was calling for radical commitment that could lead to death. Unlike the Zealots, he wasn’t calling for violent resistance but for self-sacrificial love that might include love for enemies—even Roman enemies.

The Economic Implications

Following Jesus in first-century Palestine often had immediate economic consequences. Many of his early followers were fishermen, tax collectors, and other working people who depended on their daily labor for survival. Choosing to follow an itinerant teacher meant leaving behind economic security and accepting an uncertain future.

For Matthew himself, following Jesus meant walking away from a lucrative career as a tax collector. For others, it meant leaving family businesses or abandoning plans for economic advancement. The call to discipleship wasn’t just spiritually demanding—it was economically costly.

The Social Cost of Discipleship

In a society where family and community ties were essential for survival, following Jesus often meant accepting social ostracism. Jewish followers of Jesus were frequently expelled from synagogues and cut off from their communities. Gentile converts faced similar rejection from their families and social networks.

The early Christian communities described in Acts existed partly because traditional social structures had been disrupted by the decision to follow Jesus. People who had lost their original communities needed new communities to survive and thrive.

The Religious Establishment’s Opposition

Jesus’s call to discipleship also put his followers at odds with the Jewish religious establishment. The Pharisees, Sadducees, and other religious leaders viewed Jesus’s movement as a threat to their authority and to the delicate political balance they had negotiated with Rome.

Following Jesus meant accepting the hostility of the very religious leaders who were supposed to represent God’s will to the people. This created a crisis of authority: who really spoke for God—the established religious hierarchy or this itinerant teacher from Nazareth?

The Question of Loyalty

Perhaps most challenging of all, Jesus’s call to discipleship demanded ultimate loyalty that could conflict with every other loyalty—to family, nation, economic security, and religious tradition. When he said “take up your cross,” he was asking people to be willing to die for their commitment to him and his kingdom.

This wasn’t just about personal piety or individual spiritual growth. It was about choosing sides in a cosmic conflict between the kingdom of God and the kingdoms of this world, represented most immediately by the Roman Empire and its collaborators.

The Promise of Vindication

Despite the costs, Jesus also offered hope to his original audience. His teaching about losing life to find it wasn’t just about personal transformation—it was about ultimate vindication. Those who suffered for the sake of the kingdom would be vindicated when God’s kingdom was fully established.

This promise would have been particularly meaningful to people living under occupation and oppression. Jesus was offering hope that their suffering had meaning and that justice would ultimately prevail, even if it required going through death to reach resurrection.

Greek Language Learning: Week 2, Day 3

Participles (Verbal Adjectives)

Today we’re learning about Greek participles—words that function as both verbs and adjectives. They describe ongoing actions or states and are very common in the New Testament.

Present Participles

Present participles describe ongoing or continuous action:
ἀκολουθῶν (akolouthon) - “following” (masculine singular)
ἀκολουθοῦσα (akoloutousa) - “following” (feminine singular)
ἀκολουθοῦν (akolouthoun) - “following” (neuter singular)

Participle Patterns

Active Participles (doing the action):
•Masculine: -ων (on)
•Feminine: -ουσα (ousa)
•Neuter: -ον (on)

Middle/Passive Participles (receiving the action):
•Masculine: -όμενος (omenos)
•Feminine: -ομένη (omene)
•Neuter: -όμενον (omenon)

Discipleship Participles in the New Testament

πιστεύων (pisteuon) - “believing” or “one who believes”
ἀκολουθῶν (akolouthon) - “following” or “one who follows”
μανθάνων (manthanon) - “learning” or “one who learns”

Reading Practice

ὁ ἀκολουθῶν μοι (ho akolouthon moi) “the one following me” or “he who follows me”
πᾶς ὁ πιστεύων (pas ho pisteuon) “everyone believing” or “all who believe”

Participles as Descriptions

Participles often describe the character or ongoing actions of disciples:
ὁ μαθητὴς ὁ ἀκολουθῶν
(ho mathetes ho akolouthon)
“the disciple who follows”

Memory Tip

Think of participles as “action adjectives” - they describe someone by what they’re doing:

•ἀκολουθῶν = “the following one”
•πιστεύων = “the believing one”

Practice Exercise

Identify what these participles describe:

  1. ἀκολουθῶν = ?

  1. πιστεύουσα = ?

  1. μανθάνον = ?

Answers: 1. following (masc. sing.), 2. believing (fem. sing.), 3. learning (neut. sing.)
