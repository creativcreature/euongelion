Week 3 Master Document

From Jerusalem to the Nations: Servants, Suffering, and a Surprising Conversion (Acts 6–9)

Week 3 is the turning point of the entire book of Acts.

Week 1 showed us the foundation of the early church.
Week 2 showed us what happens when the Spirit-filled church steps into public life.
Now Week 3 shows us how God grows His church through service, suffering, and surprise.

Acts 6–9 is not a quiet chapter in church history. It is bursting with movement, tension, courage, tragedy, and redemption. The gospel begins to break out of its original boundaries. Leadership expands. Persecution erupts. A martyr dies with a vision of Jesus. A persecutor meets Jesus in glory. The church is scattered — and through that scattering, the mission of God accelerates.

This week asks:
What if God uses our service, our suffering, and even our enemies to advance His mission?

Stephen becomes the first Christian to die for his witness — and the way he dies becomes the seed of a global movement.

This moment stands at the center of Acts 6–9.
It is the place where:

  • Suffering births mission.

  • Violence meets grace.

  • A persecutor sees true faith for the first time.

  • A dying man resembles his Savior.

The result?
The gospel begins to spread beyond Jerusalem for the first time.

Setting the Stage: What Happens in Acts 6–9

Below is not just a summary — each movement is expanded so the reader understands not only what happened, but why it mattered.

1. A Crisis of Care and the Birth of Deacons (Acts 6:1–7)

As the church grows, a complaint emerges: Greek-speaking widows feel neglected in the daily food distribution.
This is not a small issue — it threatens unity along ethnic and cultural lines.

The apostles respond with clarity and wisdom:

a. They refuse to neglect their calling.

The apostles say plainly that they cannot forsake “the ministry of the word and prayer.”
They understand their primary role — guiding the community spiritually — and they refuse to compromise it.

b. They elevate service to a Spirit-filled calling.

Instead of treating food distribution as “beneath them,” they insist the church choose seven men full of the Spirit and wisdom.

This teaches us a crucial truth:
Practical ministry requires spiritual maturity.
Serving tables is holy work when it is done in the name and power of the Spirit.

c. They diversify leadership.

All seven appointed leaders have Greek names — meaning the marginalized group now has empowered leadership.
The Spirit solves internal tension not by suppressing differences, but by redeeming them.

d. The result is explosive growth.

“When the Word and the care both flourish, so does the church.”

This is the first moment we see:

  • Organizational structure

  • Delegation

  • Shared leadership

It models how the church must grow — deeper and wider at the same time.

2. Stephen’s Ministry and Martyrdom (Acts 6:8–7:60)

Stephen is not an apostle.
He is not on a stage.
He is not a priest or a trained scholar.

He is a servant — and yet Acts describes him as:

  • full of faith

  • full of grace

  • full of power

  • full of the Holy Spirit

He performs signs and wonders.
He speaks with wisdom that no one can refute.
He becomes a living example that the Spirit can empower anyone, not just the original Twelve.

Stephen’s Speech (Acts 7)

Stephen retells Israel’s entire history — but this is not mere storytelling.
He is making a point:

God’s people have always resisted God’s messengers.
Abraham. Joseph. Moses. The prophets.
And now Jesus.

His speech is a theological masterpiece.
It reframes Israel’s story around Jesus as the climax of God’s plan.

His Vision

As the council condemns him, Stephen sees:

  • heaven open

  • the glory of God

  • Jesus standing at the right hand of God

In Scripture, Jesus is usually seated — His work complete.
Here, He stands.

It is the posture of an advocate, a witness, an honor-bearer.

Stephen dies with his eyes fixed on Christ — and his final words echo Christ’s own:

“Lord, receive my spirit.”
“Do not hold this sin against them.”

His death becomes the spark that lights the global mission.

3. Scattering and Expansion (Acts 8:1–25)

Stephen’s death unleashes a wave of persecution led by Saul.

Instead of crushing the movement, the persecution scatters the believers into Judea and Samaria — exactly as Jesus foretold in Acts 1:8.

Where they go, the gospel goes.

Philip in Samaria

Philip, another of the seven chosen servants, preaches in Samaria:

  • Miracles break out

  • Demons flee

  • Many believe

  • A whole city experiences joy

The gospel crosses ethnic hostility and enters a community long estranged from Jerusalem.

Simon the Sorcerer

This episode exposes the difference between:

  • fascination with power

  • and surrender to Christ

Simon wants influence, reputation, and control.
Peter rebukes him sharply — integrity matters just as much as zeal.

Samaria becomes the first major mission field.

4. The Ethiopian Eunuch (Acts 8:26–40)

The Spirit sends Philip from a thriving city ministry to a desert road.

There he meets an Ethiopian official reading Isaiah 53 — the prophecy of the suffering servant.

This man is:

  • a Gentile

  • a eunuch (excluded from full Temple access)

  • a royal treasurer

  • a seeker of God

  • returning to Africa

Philip explains the gospel “beginning with that very passage.”
The official believes, is baptized, and carries the faith back to his homeland.

This is the first documented African Christian convert in Scripture.

The gospel is no longer Jewish-only.
It is becoming a global movement.

5. Saul’s Conversion (Acts 9:1–31)

Saul is not just hostile to Christians.
He is their fiercest opponent — intelligent, zealous, influential, feared.

He is “breathing out murderous threats.”
He has legal authority to arrest believers.
He believes he is defending Israel.

Then Jesus interrupts his mission with a blinding light and a piercing question:

“Saul, Saul, why do you persecute Me?”

Jesus identifies Himself with His people — to harm them is to harm Him.

Saul is blinded, humbled, disarmed.
Ananias, a simple disciple, prays for him and calls him “brother.”

Saul rises baptized, filled with the Spirit, and preaching Christ.

This is not just a conversion story.
It is a cosmic reversal — an enemy becomes a chosen instrument.

The church, scattered and wounded, begins to heal and grow stronger.

Historical & Cultural Context (Expanded)

Hellenist vs. Hebraic Jews

The early church included:

  • Hebraic Jews – native Aramaic-speaking Jews steeped in Judean culture

  • Hellenistic Jews – Greek-speaking Jews shaped by broader Mediterranean culture

These groups had different languages, customs, and traditions — and tension was inevitable.

Acts 6 shows the Spirit building a multicultural church without erasing diversity.

Why Stephen’s Speech Enraged the Sanhedrin

Stephen challenged:

  • their stewardship of the Temple

  • their reading of Scripture

  • their resistance to God’s messengers

  • their role in rejecting the Righteous One

He reinterpreted Israel’s story around Jesus — a move that threatened the entire religious system.

They reacted not just emotionally but institutionally.

The Significance of Samaria

Jews and Samaritans shared ancestry but deep hostility.
They worshiped on different mountains.
They accused each other of corruption.

When the Spirit moves in Samaria, He is healing centuries-old division.

This is a preview of the global reconciliation the gospel brings.

Ethiopia’s Power in Antiquity

The Ethiopian eunuch served Kandake, queen of the Nubian kingdom — a wealthy and powerful African empire.

His conversion is not random — it is the gospel entering a global trade network.

Why Saul’s Conversion Was Politically Explosive

Saul trained under Gamaliel.
He was rising fast in the Pharisaic movement.
His zeal represented the future of conservative Jewish leadership.

His conversion sent shockwaves throughout Judaism.

To lose Saul was to lose a future leader.
To embrace Jesus was to embrace the very movement he tried to destroy.

Key Theological Themes (Expanded)

1. God Uses Both Service and Suffering

Stephen and Philip show us two sides of Spirit-filled ministry:

  • Service meets practical needs with sacred purpose.

  • Suffering becomes a megaphone of God’s glory.

God grows the church through deacons and martyrs, administrators and evangelists.

2. Jesus Continues His Work Through His People

The book is called ACTS for a reason — it records the acts of the risen Jesus through the Holy Spirit.

  • Jesus heals through Peter.

  • Jesus reveals Himself to Stephen.

  • Jesus confronts Saul.

  • Jesus guides Philip.

  • Jesus comforts the scattered church.

Jesus is alive, active, and involved.

3. Persecution Pushes the Gospel Forward

Persecution does not stop the church.
It clarifies it.
Purifies it.
And multiplies it.

The church does not scatter in fear but in mission.

4. The Gospel Is for Every People

Acts 6–9 touches:

  • Hellenists

  • Hebraic Jews

  • Samaritans

  • Africans

  • Pharisees

  • Roman-linked officials

The Spirit is forming a church that looks like the nations of the world.

5. No One Is Beyond the Reach of Grace

Saul’s conversion becomes the template for hope:

If God can transform Saul, He can transform anyone — including the people we’ve written off.

Linguistic Deep Dive

διάκονος (diakonos)

A servant entrusted with sacred responsibility.
This word becomes the root of “deacon” — someone who reflects Jesus, who came “not to be served, but to serve.”

μάρτυς (martys)

Originally meant “witness.”
By Stephen’s death, it becomes synonymous with “one who bears witness through suffering.”

διώκω (diōkō)

Means “to pursue, chase down, harass.”
Paul uses this same verb to later describe his pursuit of Christ — a transformation from violent pursuit to holy pursuit.

ἐκκλησία (ekklesia)

Means “called-out assembly.”
In Acts 8–9 it refers to a community no longer defined by geography but by mission.

μετανοέω (metanoeō)

“Repentance: is not just moral sorrow — it is a total change of allegiance, direction, and vision.
Saul embodies this radically.

What It Meant for Them (Expanded)

1. Leadership had to grow if the mission was going to grow.

The Spirit expands leadership beyond the apostles — a pattern still needed today.

2. Suffering was normal, not exceptional.

The early church did not interpret persecution as failure, but as participation in Jesus’ own path.

3. Unity required representation, not uniformity.

The appointment of Hellenistic leaders modeled true cross-cultural reconciliation.

4. Mission was no longer tied to one city.

Jerusalem was the birthplace — but not the boundary — of the gospel.

What It Means for Us (Expanded)

1. Serving is spiritual.

When we meet needs, we join Stephen and Philip in Spirit-filled ministry.

2. Suffering may be the place where God shapes you most deeply.

God transforms the church through adversity — not around it.

3. The Spirit invites us beyond what is familiar.

Your Samaria might be a neighbor, a coworker, or a cultural barrier you have avoided.

4. The people you fear or dislike might become trophies of grace.

Never underestimate the reach of Jesus.

Cross Fitness Challenge (Week 3)

Serving, Suffering, and Surprise

Day 1 – Serve one person in a hidden, practical way.

Let service be worship.

Day 2 – Stay faithful in one difficult area.

Ask: “What obedience am I avoiding?”

Day 3 – Release one comfort that is limiting your courage.

Fear shrinks the mission. Surrender expands it.

Day 4 – Look for where God is stretching your mission field.

New people. New places. New opportunities.

Day 5 – Pray for someone far from God.

Remember Saul. No one is impossible.

Weekly Wrap-Up: The Story Turns Outward

Acts 6–9 is the hinge of the whole movement.
Through servants, through suffering, and through surprising grace, the gospel moves from:

Jerusalem → Judea → Samaria → toward the ends of the earth.

The story will only accelerate from here.
