If I truly want to follow Jesus, what does it mean to actually take up my cross in my daily life?

We’ve spent four days examining Jesus’s call to cross-bearing discipleship from multiple angles—its immediate context, its biblical connections, its meaning for the original audience, and its application to contemporary life. Now comes the moment of decision. If we’re convinced that Jesus is calling us to this kind of radical discipleship, how do we respond?

Quickstart (1 Minute Commitment)

Today’s Verse: Matthew 16:26 - “What good will it be for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul? Or what can anyone give in exchange for their soul?”

Greek Word: κερδαίνω (Kerdaino) - “To gain” or “to profit.” This is often used in commercial contexts—it’s the language of business transactions and cost-benefit analysis applied to the ultimate questions of life.

Reflection Question: How do I calculate the true cost and benefit of following Jesus versus pursuing worldly success and security?

Urgency Moment: Before this day ends, make one specific commitment that will cost you something but serve God’s kingdom. Write it down. Tell someone about it. Start tomorrow.

Prayer: “Give me wisdom to see clearly what truly matters, and courage to make decisions based on eternal rather than temporary values.”

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Six months after Dr. Michael Rodriguez arrived in Guatemala, he received an email from a colleague at Metropolitan Hospital. Dr. James Chen had been offered the position at Johns Hopkins that Michael had turned down, and he was writing to thank Michael for the opportunity. “I have to admit, I thought you were crazy when you chose Guatemala over Hopkins,” Dr. Chen wrote. “But I wanted you to know that your decision changed my perspective on what success really means.”

Dr. Chen went on to describe how Michael’s choice had sparked conversations throughout the hospital about the purpose of medicine and the meaning of a successful career. Several doctors had begun volunteering at free clinics, others had changed their research focus to address diseases affecting the global poor, and the hospital had started a new program to train doctors for international service.

Meanwhile, Michael was discovering that his own life had been transformed in ways he hadn’t anticipated. The work was indeed challenging—he was performing complex surgeries with basic equipment, training local doctors with limited resources, and living far more simply than he ever had. But he had also experienced a sense of purpose and fulfillment that had been missing from his previous success.

The breakthrough moment had come three months earlier when he successfully performed a complex cardiac procedure on a seven-year-old girl named Sofia, whose family had traveled for days to reach the clinic. As Sofia recovered and returned to her village, Michael realized that this single surgery would allow her to live a full life, attend school, and contribute to her community in ways that would have been impossible without the intervention.

“I thought I was giving up my career to help people in Guatemala,” Michael wrote in his journal that night. “What I discovered was that I had found my true calling and learned what it means to practice medicine as a vocation rather than just a profession. The life I thought I was losing was actually the life I was finding.”

Takeaway: Sometimes what we think we’re sacrificing turns out to be what was keeping us from discovering our true purpose and deepest fulfillment.

Counting the Cost

Jesus’s question about gaining the world but forfeiting your soul isn’t rhetorical—it’s a call to serious cost-benefit analysis. Following Jesus requires honestly assessing what we might need to give up and what we stand to gain, both in this life and in eternity.

The “cost” of discipleship might include:
•Comfort and security that comes from pursuing conventional success
•Approval from others who don’t understand our choices
•Financial advantages that come from prioritizing profit over principle
•Time and energy that could be spent on personal pleasure or advancement
•Control over our own agenda and priorities

The “benefit” of discipleship includes:
•Alignment with our deepest purpose and calling
•Peace that comes from living with integrity
•Joy that comes from serving something greater than ourselves
•Community with others who share similar values and commitments
•Confidence about our eternal destiny and ultimate meaning

Starting Where You Are

Not everyone is called to make dramatic changes like Dr. Rodriguez’s move to Guatemala. Cross-bearing discipleship begins with honestly assessing where you are and taking the next faithful step, whatever that might be.

This might involve:

Small Daily Choices: Looking for opportunities each day to choose service over self-interest, generosity over accumulation, truth over convenience, or love over indifference.

Gradual Lifestyle Adjustments: Making incremental changes toward simpler living, more generous giving, or more intentional relationships, allowing these changes to build momentum over time.

Specific Commitments: Identifying one or two areas where you sense God calling you to greater faithfulness and making concrete commitments to growth in those areas.

Practical Steps for Cross-Bearing

Financial Discipleship:
•Establish a giving plan that requires some sacrifice and trust
•Make consumer decisions based on kingdom values rather than just personal preference
•Consider career choices based on calling and contribution rather than just compensation
•Practice contentment with “enough” rather than always wanting more

Relational Discipleship:
•Prioritize relationships that matter most, even when this requires saying no to other opportunities
•Practice forgiveness and reconciliation, even when it’s difficult or costly
•Look for ways to serve others, particularly those who can’t repay you
•Engage in difficult conversations when truth and love require it

Vocational Discipleship:
•Approach your work as a calling to serve God and others, not just a way to earn money
•Look for ways to use your skills and resources for kingdom purposes
•Consider how your career contributes to human flourishing and God’s purposes in the world
•Be willing to make professional choices based on ethical considerations

Community Discipleship:
•Participate actively in Christian community for mutual encouragement and accountability
•Engage in service projects and social justice initiatives
•Share your resources with others in need
•Mentor others who are learning to follow Jesus

Dealing with Fear and Resistance

Most of us experience fear when we consider what cross-bearing discipleship might require of us. This is normal and doesn’t disqualify us from following Jesus. The key is to acknowledge our fears honestly while not allowing them to prevent us from taking faithful steps.

Common fears include:
•Financial insecurity if we’re more generous or make career choices based on calling rather than just income
•Social rejection if we live differently from cultural norms
•Missing out on opportunities if we prioritize kingdom values over personal advancement
•Being taken advantage of if we’re too generous or forgiving

These fears often reflect deeper questions about whether we can really trust God to provide for us and whether following Jesus will ultimately lead to blessing or deprivation.

The Role of Community and Accountability

Cross-bearing discipleship is difficult to sustain in isolation. We need relationships with others who share similar commitments and can encourage us when the cost feels overwhelming.

This might involve:
•Finding or creating a small group focused on discipleship and spiritual growth
•Identifying a mentor who models the kind of life you want to live
•Participating in service projects with other Christians
•Being part of a church community that takes discipleship seriously

Growing into Greater Faithfulness

Cross-bearing discipleship is a lifelong journey rather than a one-time decision. We grow into greater faithfulness gradually, as we learn to trust God more deeply and experience the joy that comes from living according to his purposes.

This process involves:
•Regular self-examination and confession when we fall short
•Celebrating progress and growth, even when it’s incremental
•Learning from failures and setbacks rather than being paralyzed by them
•Continually asking God to show us the next step in following Jesus more faithfully

The Promise of Finding Life

Jesus’s promise that those who lose their life for his sake will find it isn’t just about the afterlife—it’s about discovering authentic life in the present. Many people who have made significant sacrifices to follow Jesus report that they’ve found greater fulfillment, purpose, and joy than they experienced when they were living primarily for themselves.

This doesn’t mean that cross-bearing discipleship is always easy or pleasant, but it does mean that it leads to the kind of life that is ultimately satisfying and meaningful.

Greek Language Learning: Week 2, Day 5

Infinitives and Purpose Clauses

Today we’re learning about Greek infinitives—verbal nouns that express purpose, result, or indirect commands. Jesus often uses infinitives to explain why he calls us to discipleship.

Infinitive Forms

Infinitives are the “to” form of verbs:
ἀκολουθεῖν (akolouthein) - “to follow”
πιστεύειν (pisteuein) - “to believe”
σῴζειν (sozein) - “to save”
ἀπολλύναι (apollynai) - “to lose” or “to destroy”

Infinitive Endings

Present Active: -ειν (ein)
Present Middle/Passive: -εσθαι (esthai)
Aorist Active: -σαι (sai)
Aorist Middle: -σασθαι (sasthai)

Purpose Clauses with Infinitives

ἦλθον καλέσαι (elthon kalesai) “I came to call” or “I came in order to call”
θέλει σῶσαι (thelei sosai) “he wants to save”

Jesus’s Teaching with Infinitives

ὃς γὰρ ἐὰν θέλῃ τὴν ψυχὴν αὐτοῦ σῶσαι ἀπολέσει αὐτήν
(hos gar ean thele ten psychen autou sosai apolesei auten)
“For whoever wants to save his life will lose it”

Breaking down the infinitive:
•σῶσαι (sosai) - “to save” (aorist active infinitive)

Common Infinitive Constructions

Purpose: “in order to...” ἦλθεν σῶσαι = “he came to save”

Indirect Command: “to do something” κελεύει ἀκολουθεῖν = “he commands (them) to follow”

Result: “so as to...” ὥστε σῴζειν = “so as to save”

Key Infinitive Vocabulary

σῴζω (sozo) - “I save”
σῶσαι (sosai) - “to save”
ἀπόλλυμι (apollymi) - “I destroy/lose”
ἀπολέσαι (apolesai) - “to lose/destroy”
εὑρίσκω (heurisko) - “I find”
εὑρεῖν (heurein) - “to find”

Reading Practice

τί ὠφελεῖται ἄνθρωπος ἐὰν κερδήσῃ τὸν κόσμον ὅλον
(ti opheletai anthropos ean kerdese ton kosmon holon)
“What does it profit a person if he gains the whole world”

Memory Tip

Infinitives often end in -ειν (like our “to” + verb). Think “Ein” = “In order to”!

Practice Exercise

Identify the infinitives:

  1. ἀκολουθεῖν = ?

  1. πιστεύειν = ?

  1. σῶσαι = ?

Answers: 1. to follow, 2. to believe, 3. to save
