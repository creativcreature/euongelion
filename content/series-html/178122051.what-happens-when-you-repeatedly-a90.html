How do I maintain hope and continue pressing forward when transformation seems slow and setbacks are frequent?

We conclude our week by examining Paul’s perspective on the lifelong process of spiritual growth. When we’re dealing with persistent struggles, it’s easy to become discouraged by the slow pace of change or devastated by unexpected setbacks. Paul’s teaching provides a framework for maintaining hope and continuing to press forward even when perfection seems impossible. The key is understanding that transformation is a process, not a destination, and that our identity is secure even while our behavior is still being transformed.

Day 6: Review and Practice

“Not that I have already obtained all this, or have already arrived at my goal, but I press on to take hold of that for which Christ Jesus took hold of me.” - Philippians 3:12

Quickstart (1 Minute Commitment)

Today’s Verse: Philippians 3:12 - “Not that I have already obtained all this, or have already arrived at my goal, but I press on to take hold of that for which Christ Jesus took hold of me.”

Greek Word: διώκω (Dioko) - “To pursue” or “to press on.” This word was used for hunting or chasing something with intense focus and determination. Paul pursues spiritual maturity with the same intensity a hunter pursues prey.

Reflection Question: What would it look like to “press on” in my areas of persistent struggle with the same determination Paul describes, while maintaining realistic expectations about the process?

Urgency Moment: Stop expecting perfection and start embracing process. Choose one area of repeated failure and commit to pressing on with patient determination rather than giving up in discouragement.

Prayer: “Help me press on toward transformation with hope and determination, trusting that you who began a good work in me will complete it.”

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Two years after his journey toward recovery began, Marcus Thompson stood at the podium in his church, preparing to share his testimony during a special service on God’s grace. As he looked out at the congregation, he saw faces that represented different stages of his own journey—some struggling with hidden addictions, others questioning whether God could really change them, and still others wondering if their repeated failures disqualified them from serving God.

“Two years ago,” Marcus began, “I would have told you that victory over sin meant never failing again. I thought that if I were really changed, if God had really saved me, I would stop struggling with gambling. When I continued to fail, I concluded that either God wasn’t real or I wasn’t really saved.”

Marcus paused, remembering the shame and hopelessness he had felt during those dark months. “But I’ve learned that victory doesn’t look like I thought it would. Two years ago, when I gambled, I would stay until the casino closed, lose everything I could access, lie to my wife about where I’d been, and then avoid church and Christian friends for weeks because of shame. Last month, when I faced the strongest temptation I’ve experienced in over a year, I gambled for about an hour, lost fifty dollars, immediately left, told my wife what happened before she asked, called my pastor the next morning, and was back in church that Sunday.”

The congregation was silent, hanging on every word. Marcus continued, “Some of you might think, ‘But you still gambled. Where’s the victory in that?’ Here’s what I’ve learned: victory isn’t the absence of struggle—it’s the transformation of how we struggle. God’s power isn’t most evident when we never fail but when our failures become smaller, shorter, and lead us back to him faster than before.”

Marcus opened his Bible to Philippians 3. “Paul says he hasn’t ‘already obtained’ or ‘already arrived,’ but he presses on. That’s become my motto. I haven’t arrived at sinless perfection, but I’m pressing on. And the direction of my life, the trajectory of my growth, the pattern of my responses—all of that has been transformed by God’s grace.”

As Marcus concluded his testimony, he noticed Jake in the third row, the young man he had mentored through his own gambling addiction. Jake was now six months into recovery and had recently started leading a support group for other men struggling with addiction. Next to Jake sat Mrs. Henderson, the woman Marcus had visited in the hospital during his moment of crisis two years earlier. She had become like a grandmother to Marcus’s family and often reminded him how God had used his visit during her darkest hour to restore her hope.

“I want to close with this,” Marcus said. “If you’re struggling with repeated sin, if you feel like you’re failing God over and over again, I want you to know that your struggle doesn’t disqualify you from his love or from serving him. In fact, it might be preparing you to help others who face the same battles. God’s power is made perfect in weakness, and sometimes our greatest ministry comes from our deepest struggles.”

As Marcus stepped down from the podium, he realized that his gambling addiction, which he had once seen as his greatest shame, had become one of the primary ways God was using him to serve others. The very area of his repeated failure had become a source of hope and healing for people like Jake and countless others who needed to know that transformation was possible even when it was slow and imperfect.

Takeaway: God can use our areas of persistent struggle as sources of ministry to others, and transformation is measured not by the absence of failure but by the direction of growth and the pattern of response to failure.

Deep Dive (15 Minutes)

Practical Implementation Focus:
Maintaining Hope in the Process of Transformation

Full Passage: Philippians 3:12-16 (NIV)
12 Not that I have already obtained all this, or have already arrived at my goal, but I press on to take hold of that for which Christ Jesus took hold of me. 13 Brothers and sisters, I do not consider myself yet to have taken hold of it. But one thing I do: Forgetting what is behind and straining toward what is ahead, 14 I press on toward the goal to win the prize for which God has called me heavenward in Christ Jesus.15 All of us, then, who are mature should take such a view of things. And if on some point you think differently, that too God will make clear to you. 16 Only let us live up to what we have already attained.

The Context of Paul’s Pursuit

Paul’s discussion of pressing on comes in the context of his testimony about his former life and his current priorities. He had just described his impressive religious credentials and his decision to count them as “loss” compared to knowing Christ. Now he addresses the ongoing nature of spiritual growth and the need for persistent pursuit of spiritual maturity.

This passage is particularly relevant for believers struggling with repeated sin because it acknowledges that even the apostle Paul hadn’t “arrived” at spiritual perfection but was still in the process of growth and transformation.

The Honesty of “Not Yet”

Paul begins with remarkable honesty: “Not that I have already obtained all this, or have already arrived at my goal” (v. 12). This admission is significant for several reasons:

Realistic Expectations: Even the apostle Paul, who had extraordinary spiritual experiences and wrote much of the New Testament, acknowledged that he hadn’t achieved spiritual perfection.

Ongoing Process: Spiritual growth is presented as a lifelong process rather than a one-time achievement or a quick fix.

Permission to Struggle: Paul’s honesty gives other believers permission to acknowledge their own ongoing struggles without shame or despair.

Hope in Process: The fact that Paul was still growing suggests that progress is possible even when perfection isn’t achieved.

The Metaphor of Athletic Pursuit

Paul uses athletic imagery throughout this passage, drawing on the Greek games that were familiar to his readers. The metaphor of pressing on (διώκω) suggests several important aspects of spiritual growth:

Intense Focus: Athletes pursue their goals with single-minded determination, not allowing distractions to derail their efforts.

Sustained Effort: Athletic achievement requires consistent training over long periods, not just occasional bursts of activity.

Clear Objective: Athletes have specific goals they’re working toward, not vague aspirations for general improvement.

Acceptance of Process: Athletes understand that improvement comes gradually through consistent effort, not through instant transformation.

Taking Hold of Christ’s Purpose

Paul’s goal is “to take hold of that for which Christ Jesus took hold of me” (v. 12). This phrase suggests several important truths:

Divine Initiative: Christ took hold of Paul first—salvation and calling are God’s initiative, not human achievement.

Personal Purpose: Christ had a specific purpose in mind when he called Paul, and Paul’s goal is to fulfill that purpose.

Reciprocal Response: Paul seeks to take hold of what Christ has already taken hold of—there’s a cooperation between divine calling and human response.

Ongoing Discovery: Paul is still discovering and pursuing the full implications of what Christ called him to be and do.

Old Testament Connection

Paul’s concept of pressing on toward a goal echoes several Old Testament themes, particularly the imagery of the Israelites’ journey toward the Promised Land. Like the Israelites, believers are on a journey from where they were (slavery to sin) to where God is calling them (spiritual maturity and ultimate glorification).

The book of Hebrews explicitly makes this connection, describing the Christian life as a journey toward a “better country” and encouraging believers to “run with perseverance the race marked out for us” (Hebrews 12:1). This Old Testament background helps us understand that struggle and gradual progress are normal parts of the spiritual journey, not signs of failure.

The Discipline of Forgetting

Paul describes his strategy: “Forgetting what is behind and straining toward what is ahead” (v. 13). This “forgetting” is not amnesia but a deliberate choice to not be controlled by past failures or achievements:

Past Failures: Paul chooses not to be paralyzed by guilt over his persecution of Christians or discouraged by ongoing struggles with sin.

Past Achievements: Paul also chooses not to rest on his laurels or become complacent about his spiritual accomplishments.

Present Focus: By forgetting what is behind, Paul can focus his energy on present opportunities and future growth.

Forward Momentum: This forgetting creates psychological and spiritual space for continued progress rather than being stuck in the past.

The Intensity of Straining

The word Paul uses for “straining” (ἐπεκτείνομαι) suggests the intense physical effort of a runner stretching toward the finish line. This imagery conveys several important aspects of spiritual pursuit:

Maximum Effort: Spiritual growth requires our best effort, not casual or half-hearted attempts.

Uncomfortable Stretching: Growth often involves moving beyond our comfort zones and natural inclinations.

Focused Direction: Like a runner straining toward the finish line, believers should have clear spiritual objectives.

Sustained Intensity: This straining is not a one-time burst but a sustained attitude throughout the race.

The Goal and the Prize

Paul describes pressing on “toward the goal to win the prize for which God has called me heavenward in Christ Jesus” (v. 14). This goal has several dimensions:

Ultimate Glorification: The final prize is complete transformation and eternal fellowship with Christ.

Present Sanctification: The ongoing goal is increasing conformity to Christ’s character and purposes.

Heavenly Calling: The goal is not earthly success but spiritual maturity and divine approval.

Christ-Centered Focus: The goal is not self-improvement but becoming more like Christ and fulfilling his purposes.

The Mark of Maturity

Paul suggests that this perspective on spiritual growth is a mark of maturity: “All of us, then, who are mature should take such a view of things” (v. 15). Spiritual maturity includes:

Realistic Self-Assessment: Mature believers acknowledge their ongoing need for growth rather than claiming to have arrived.

Persistent Pursuit: Maturity involves continued effort toward spiritual goals rather than complacency or giving up.

Process Orientation: Mature believers focus on the direction and consistency of growth rather than demanding instant perfection.

Grace-Based Motivation: The pursuit is motivated by gratitude for what Christ has done rather than by trying to earn God’s favor.

Dealing with Different Perspectives

Paul acknowledges that believers may have different perspectives on some aspects of spiritual growth: “And if on some point you think differently, that too God will make clear to you” (v. 15). This suggests:

Unity in Essentials: While there may be differences in secondary matters, all mature believers should share the basic commitment to continued growth.

Divine Illumination: God will provide clarity on disputed points as believers continue to seek him and grow in maturity.

Patience with Process: Both personal growth and community understanding develop over time rather than instantly.

Humility in Differences: Believers should maintain humility about their own understanding while remaining committed to growth.

Living Up to Current Attainment

Paul concludes with the principle: “Only let us live up to what we have already attained” (v. 16). This suggests several important applications:

Stewardship of Growth: We’re responsible to live consistently with the spiritual maturity we’ve already achieved.

No Regression: While we may not be perfect, we shouldn’t deliberately move backward from progress we’ve already made.

Building on Foundation: New growth should build on previous spiritual development rather than starting over repeatedly.

Practical Consistency: Spiritual growth should be evident in practical life choices and behavior patterns.

Applications for Persistent Sin

Paul’s teaching provides several practical applications for believers dealing with repeated failures in the same areas:

Maintain Long-term Perspective: Focus on the overall direction of growth rather than being devastated by individual failures.

Embrace the Process: Accept that transformation takes time and involves setbacks rather than expecting instant perfection.

Learn from Failure: Use failures as opportunities to learn better strategies and develop greater dependence on God’s grace.

Celebrate Progress: Acknowledge and celebrate genuine progress even when it falls short of complete victory.

Stay in the Race: Don’t quit pursuing growth just because progress is slower or more difficult than expected.

The Role of Community

While Paul uses individual language (”I press on”), the context suggests that this pursuit happens within Christian community:

Shared Journey: Other believers are on the same journey and can provide encouragement and support.

Mutual Accountability: Community can help us maintain focus on our spiritual goals and live up to what we’ve attained.

Diverse Gifts: Different believers may have insights and strengths that help others in their areas of struggle.

Collective Maturity: The church as a whole grows in maturity as individual members pursue spiritual growth.

Practical Strategies for Pressing On

Based on Paul’s teaching and the broader context of Scripture, several practical strategies emerge for maintaining hope and progress in areas of persistent struggle:

Set Realistic Goals: Focus on progress rather than perfection, and celebrate small victories along the way.

Develop Support Systems: Cultivate relationships with other believers who can provide encouragement, accountability, and practical help.

Practice Regular Self-Examination: Regularly assess your spiritual condition and growth patterns, looking for evidence of God’s work even in small changes.

Maintain Spiritual Disciplines: Consistent prayer, Bible study, worship, and service create conditions where God’s transforming power can work more freely.

Seek Professional Help: For some persistent struggles, professional counseling or medical treatment may be part of God’s provision for healing and growth.

Focus on Service: Often our struggles are transformed as we focus on serving others rather than on our own problems.

The Ultimate Hope

Paul’s teaching points to ultimate hope that transcends our current struggles:

Divine Completion: God who began the work of salvation will complete it (Philippians 1:6).

Future Transformation: Complete transformation awaits us in the resurrection and eternal life with Christ.

Present Power: The same power that will complete our transformation is available now to help us grow and change.

Secure Identity: Our identity and security are based on what Christ has done, not on our performance or progress.

Optional Journal Reflection

  1. How do you balance realistic expectations about the slow process of change with genuine effort to grow and overcome persistent struggles? What helps you maintain hope when progress seems slow?

  2. What does “pressing on” look like practically in your areas of repeated failure? What specific goals and strategies can help you continue pursuing growth without becoming discouraged?

  3. How can you better utilize Christian community to support your long-term spiritual growth? What role should other believers play in helping you maintain perspective and hope?

Share Your Thoughts

How does Paul’s honesty about not having “already obtained” or “already arrived” encourage you in your own spiritual journey? What does it mean to you to “press on” in areas where you continue to struggle? Share your thoughts on how to maintain hope and determination in the lifelong process of transformation.

Greek Language Learning: Week 4, Day 6

The Language of Pressing On and Perseverance

Today we complete our fourth week of Greek by learning vocabulary related to pursuit, perseverance, and the ongoing process of spiritual growth.

Pursuit and Achievement Language

διώκω (dioko) - “I pursue” or “I press on”
καταλαμβάνω (katalambano) - “I obtain” or “I take hold of”
φθάνω (phthano) - “I arrive” or “I attain”

οὐχ ὅτι ἤδη ἔλαβον ἢ ἤδη τετελείωμαι
(ouch hoti ede elabon e ede teteleiοmai)
“Not that I have already obtained or am already perfected”

The Language of Completion and Perfection

τελειόω (teleioo) - “I perfect” or “I complete”
τέλειος (teleios) - “perfect” or “mature”
τελείωσις (teleiosis) - “perfection” or “completion”

Athletic and Racing Vocabulary

τρέχω (trecho) - “I run” δρόμος (dromos) - “race” or “course”
στάδιον (stadion) - “stadium” or “race course”
βραβεῖον (brabeion) - “prize”
στέφανος (stephanos) - “crown” or “wreath”

The Language of Forgetting and Straining

ἐπιλανθάνομαι (epilanthanomai) - “I forget”
ἐπεκτείνομαι (epekteinomai) - “I stretch out” or “I strain toward”

τὰ μὲν ὀπίσω ἐπιλανθανόμενος
(ta men opiso epilanthanomenos)
“forgetting what lies behind”

τοῖς δὲ ἔμπροσθεν ἐπεκτεινόμενος
(tois de emprosthen epekteinomenos)
“straining toward what lies ahead”

Direction and Position Language

ὀπίσω (opiso) - “behind” or “backward”
ἔμπροσθεν (emprosthen) - “ahead” or “forward”
σκοπός (skopos) - “goal” or “target”

Calling and Purpose Language

κλῆσις (klesis) - “calling”
καλέω (kaleo) - “I call”
ἄνω (ano) - “upward” or “above”

τῆς ἄνω κλήσεως τοῦ θεοῦ ἐν Χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ
(tes ano kleseos tou theou en Christo Iesou)
“the upward calling of God in Christ Jesus”

Maturity and Growth Language

τέλειος (teleios) - “mature” or “perfect”
φρονέω (phroneo) - “I think” or “I have an attitude”
ἀποκαλύπτω (apokalypto) - “I reveal”

ὅσοι οὖν τέλειοι, τοῦτο φρονῶμεν
(hosoi oun teleioi, touto phronomen)
“Therefore, as many as are mature, let us have this attitude”

The Language of Attainment and Progress

φθάνω (phthano) - “I arrive at” or “I attain”
στοιχέω (stoicheo) - “I walk in line” or “I keep in step”

εἰς ὃ ἐφθάσαμεν, τῷ αὐτῷ στοιχεῖν
(eis ho ephthasamen, to auto stoichein)
“to what we have attained, let us walk by the same”

Reading Practice

διώκω δὲ εἰ καὶ καταλάβω, ἐφ’ ᾧ καὶ κατελήμφθην ὑπὸ Χριστοῦ Ἰησοῦ
(dioko de ei kai katalabo, eph’ ho kai katelemphten hypo Christou Iesou)
“But I press on, if I may also lay hold of that for which I was laid hold of by Christ Jesus”

The Grammar of Ongoing Process

Present Tense Verbs: διώκω (dioko) shows ongoing, continuous action
Conditional Particles: εἰ καί (ei kai) - “if indeed” or “if perhaps”
Passive Voice: κατελήμφθην (katelemphten) - “I was laid hold of” (by Christ)

Participles of Manner

ἐπιλανθανόμενος (epilanthanomenos) - “forgetting” (present participle showing ongoing action)
ἐπεκτεινόμενος (epekteinomenos) - “straining” (present participle showing continuous effort)

The One Thing Focus

ἓν δὲ (hen de) - “but one thing”
This phrase shows Paul’s singular focus and priority

Memory Tip

διώκω (dioko) can mean both “pursue” (positive) and “persecute” (negative) - Paul went from persecuting Christ to pursuing Christ!

Weeks Conclusion

Over the past six days, we’ve explored one of the most challenging aspects of the Christian life: what happens when we repeatedly do the same sin. We’ve learned that:

Day 1: Even the apostle Paul struggled with internal conflict between wanting to do good and failing to do it consistently.

Day 2: God’s promise of forgiveness is available for repeated failures, but genuine confession involves agreeing with God about our sin and depending on his grace for transformation.

Day 3: Grace doesn’t give us license to continue in sin but transforms our relationship to sin, making us people who are grieved by failure rather than comfortable with it.

Day 4: God’s power is often most evident not in the elimination of our struggles but in how those struggles are gradually transformed and how we respond to failure differently over time.

Day 5: Victory comes through learning to walk in dependence on the Holy Spirit’s power rather than trying to overcome sin through willpower alone.

Day 6: Transformation is a lifelong process that includes setbacks and ongoing struggles, but we can maintain hope by focusing on the direction of growth rather than demanding instant perfection.

The story of Marcus Thompson illustrates how God’s grace works in the real world—not by instantly removing all struggles but by gradually transforming how we understand and respond to those struggles. His journey from shame and secrecy to honesty and hope, from trying to manage his addiction to being transformed by God’s power, shows us what it looks like to press on in the process of sanctification.

Remember: your struggles with repeated sin don’t disqualify you from God’s love or from serving him. In fact, they may be preparing you to help others who face similar battles. God’s power is made perfect in weakness, and sometimes our greatest ministry comes from our deepest struggles.

Keep pressing on. The God who began a good work in you will complete it.
