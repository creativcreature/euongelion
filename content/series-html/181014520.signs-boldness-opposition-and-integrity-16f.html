Day 2: Acts 3 – The Message Behind the Miracle

“Repent, then, and turn to God, so that your sins may be wiped out, that times of refreshing may come from the Lord.” – Acts 3:19 (NIV)

Quickstart (1–3 Minute Commitment)

Today’s Passage: Acts 3:11–26 – Peter’s sermon in Solomon’s Colonnade.
The man who was just healed clings to Peter and John, crowds rush in, and Peter does not take credit. He redirects all attention to Jesus: the Holy and Righteous One, the Author of Life, the fulfillment of God’s promises.

Greek Word:
μετάνοια (metanoia) – “repentance,” not just regret but a total reorientation of life toward God.
Peter is not shaming the crowd. He is inviting them into refreshing (v. 19) — spiritual renewal, restoration, and the arrival of God’s Kingdom in their lives.

What’s Happening in Acts 3?

A miracle has occurred, and everyone is stunned. Peter immediately makes three crucial moves:

  1. He refuses personal glory.
    He says, “Why do you stare at us as if we made this man walk?” The miracle is Jesus’ work, not theirs.

  2. He confronts their participation in rejecting Jesus.
    Peter names the truth: they handed over the Author of Life.

  3. He offers hope, not condemnation.
    “You acted in ignorance… turn to God… so that times of refreshing may come.”

This is one of the most balanced sermons in Scripture:
truth without cruelty, conviction without despair, confrontation with an invitation.

Peter interprets the miracle as a sign pointing to Jesus — not a spectacle, but a signal that God is restoring all things (v. 21).

Reflection Question

Where is God inviting me to turn toward Him instead of explaining away His work in my life?

Urgency Moment

Set aside 3 minutes today to ask:
“Lord, where are You refreshing my life, and where are You calling me to repent?”
Don’t rush past either one.

Prayer

“Jesus, help me see Your work clearly and respond honestly. Where I need conviction, give it gently. Where I need refreshing, bring it fully. Teach me to point others to You, not to myself. Amen.”

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Part 2 of a 5-Day True Story: A Message That Confronts and Invites

After Wilberforce’s conversion, he could no longer look at the world the same way. But clarity brought conflict. The more he learned about the slave trade, the more he realized that the entire British economy — shipping, insurance, ports, plantations — was built on suffering. Calling for its end would challenge powerful people, and powerful people do not enjoy being challenged.

Yet, like Peter in Solomon’s Colonnade, Wilberforce knew he could not stay silent.

In 1787, he made his first public attempt to expose the truth. He delivered speeches in Parliament describing the cruelty of the Middle Passage: how enslaved Africans were packed into ships so tightly they could barely move, how disease spread in the dark holds, how death was so common that sharks followed the ships for miles. Many members of Parliament shifted uncomfortably, not because they doubted him — but because they already knew, and preferred not to be reminded.

Predictably, backlash came swiftly. Influential businessmen ridiculed him. Newspapers mocked him. Some members of Parliament whispered that he was young, naive, religiously zealous — “unreasonable.” His opponents labeled him unpatriotic, accusing him of weakening Britain’s global power. Behind closed doors, they told him he would ruin his career.

Wilberforce knew the cost of speaking publicly. Yet he also knew something deeper:
Truth is not a weapon. It is an invitation.
Just as Peter confronted the crowd not to shame them but to offer repentance and refreshing, Wilberforce exposed the horrors of the slave trade to call his nation toward humanity, justice, and spiritual renewal.

He wrote in his journal that year:
“So enormous, so dreadful did its wickedness appear that my own mind was completely made up. Let the consequences be what they would — I from this time determined that I would never rest until I had effected its abolition.”

Opposition did not silence him. It clarified him.

Takeaway:
When God calls you to speak truth, some will resist, some will listen, and some will change. Your job is not to manage reactions — only to witness faithfully.

Dive Further (5 Minutes)

Peter’s sermon in Acts 3 is a model for Spirit-filled public witness:

• It is courageous but compassionate.
• It tells the truth about sin without dehumanizing the sinner.
• It centers the work of Jesus, not the personality of the preacher.
• It offers repentance as a gift, not a punishment.
• It announces “times of refreshing” — a phrase unique in the New Testament.

In Peter’s view, repentance is not merely turning from sin — it is turning toward renewal.
