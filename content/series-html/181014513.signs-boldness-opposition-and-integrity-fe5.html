Day 1: Acts 3 — A Sign at the Beautiful Gate

“Then Peter said, ‘Silver or gold I do not have, but what I do have I give you.
In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, walk.’”
Acts 3:6 (NIV)

Quickstart (1–3 Minute Commitment)

Today’s Passage:
Acts 3:1–10 — Peter and John encounter a man who has been lame from birth. He expects money. Instead, he receives healing in the name of Jesus.

Greek Word:
ὄνομα (ónoma) — “name; authority.”
In the ancient world, a person’s “name” meant their identity, character, and legal authority. When Peter says, “In the name of Jesus,” he is not performing a religious formula. He is invoking the living authority of the risen Christ.

What’s Happening in Acts 3?
Two ordinary apostles walk into the Temple for the afternoon prayer service. One extraordinary moment changes everything:

A beggar looks for coins.
Peter offers Christ.
A body is restored.
A crowd is awakened.
A sermon is birthed.
The church steps into the public eye.

This miracle is not merely compassionate — it is strategic. It is a sign that the ministry of Jesus has not ended. It has expanded.

Reflection Question:

Where am I offering people small solutions when God might be inviting me to offer the presence and authority of Jesus instead?

Urgency Moment:

Today, ask God for one opportunity to offer something deeper than advice, positivity, or “silver and gold.” Pray:
“Jesus, give me the courage to offer You.”

Prayer:

“Lord Jesus, Your name carries power, compassion, and authority. Forgive me when I rely on my own resources instead of Your presence. Teach me to see interruptions as invitations, and to give others what You’ve given me — Yourself.” Amen.

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Part 1 of a 5-Day True Story: The Birth of an Unlikely Movement

In 1785, a 26-year-old British politician named William Wilberforce sat alone on a hilltop in the English countryside, wrestling with a conviction he could neither escape nor explain. He had recently experienced what he called “the great change” — a sincere conversion to Christianity that reoriented his entire understanding of purpose and responsibility. But now he faced a terrifying question:

Could he remain in Parliament and still follow Christ wholeheartedly?

At that time, politics was known for bribery, ego, and ruthless ambition. Many new believers abandoned public life to embrace private devotion. Wilberforce wondered if he must do the same.

Unsure, he sought counsel from the aging former slave-ship captain turned Anglican minister John Newton, author of Amazing Grace. Newton told him something shocking:
“God has raised you up for this very thing. Stay in Parliament. Serve Him there.”

It was in the wake of that conversation that Wilberforce learned about the horrors of the transatlantic slave trade — the overcrowded ships, the brutal conditions, the staggering loss of life. He wrote in his journal shortly after:

“God Almighty has set before me two great objects:
the suppression of the slave trade and the reformation of manners.”

He had no plan, no strategy, no idea how fierce the opposition would be.
What he had was conviction — a sense that God was calling him into public witness, not private retreat.

At that moment, Wilberforce felt wildly unqualified. The political system was stacked against him. The slave trade was economically entrenched. The opposition would be powerful. But what he did next mirrored the pattern we see in Acts:

He gathered a small group of believers.
They prayed.
They sought the Spirit’s guidance.
They committed themselves to truth, integrity, and courage.

Before the speeches, before the campaigns, before the votes — the movement began in quiet rooms, in prayer, in the company of a handful of people who believed Jesus still acted through His followers.

Takeaway:
Every public work of God begins in hidden places — in conviction, prayer, obedience, and the courage to say yes before you know the cost.

Dive Further (5 Minutes)

Acts 3 isn’t just a miracle story. It’s a theological statement.

1. The Temple is no longer the only place where God acts.

The miracle happens in the Temple courts, but the power does not come from the Temple system. It comes from Jesus — the new and better Temple.

2. The apostles continue the ministry of Jesus.

Compare Acts 3 with Luke 5 and Luke 7.
The pattern is identical: a hurting person, a healing, a crowd, and a message.
Luke is telling you: Jesus is still working — just through different hands.

3. Miracles create gospel opportunity, not entertainment.

Peter immediately redirects the crowd:
“This is not about us. This is about Jesus.”
Signs are not the point.
They point to the point.

4. The healing symbolizes salvation itself.

Lameness meant exclusion from full Temple participation.
Healing meant restoration to community, worship, and identity.
That is what salvation is — restoration.
