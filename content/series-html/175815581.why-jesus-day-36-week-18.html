What did “Savior” mean to people living under Roman rule, and why would this announcement have been both hopeful and dangerous?

Today we’re stepping into the world of Luke’s original readers. What did it mean to hear about a “Savior” being born when Caesar Augustus was already being hailed as the “Savior of the world”? How would both Jews and Gentiles have understood the angels’ announcement, and why might it have gotten people into trouble with the authorities?

Day 3: What’s the Lesson for the Original Audience?

“Today in the town of David a Savior has been born to you; he is the Messiah, the Lord.” - Luke 2:11

Quickstart (1 Minute Commitment)

Today’s Verse: Luke 2:11 - “Today in the town of David a Savior has been born to you; he is the Messiah, the Lord.”

Latin Word: Caesar Augustus was officially titled “Divi Filius” (Son of God) and “Soter” (Savior). The angel’s announcement uses the same terminology that the Roman Empire applied to its emperor, creating an implicit challenge to imperial authority.

Reflection Question: What would it have meant for first-century people to hear that a baby in an obscure village was the true “Savior” when the most powerful man in the world already claimed that title?

Prayer: “Help me understand the courage it would have taken for early Christians to proclaim Jesus as Lord in a world where Caesar demanded ultimate allegiance.”

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Imagine you’re living in a country under foreign occupation. The occupying power has brought some benefits—better roads, more efficient government, relative peace—but at the cost of your national independence and cultural identity. The foreign ruler is praised in official propaganda as a great benefactor, a bringer of peace, even a divine figure worthy of worship.

Then you hear a rumor that somewhere in your country, a child has been born who is the true liberator, the rightful ruler, the one who will restore your nation’s freedom and dignity. This news would be simultaneously thrilling and terrifying. Thrilling because it offers hope for real change; terrifying because believing it—much less spreading it—could be considered treason.

This captures something of what Luke’s original audience would have felt when they heard about Jesus’s birth. The Roman Empire had brought unprecedented peace and prosperity to the Mediterranean world, but at the cost of local autonomy and traditional values. Caesar Augustus was widely celebrated as a savior figure who had ended decades of civil war and established the Pax Romana (Roman Peace).

Into this context comes the announcement that the true Savior has been born—not in Rome’s palaces, but in an obscure Jewish village. Not to bring more efficient administration, but to establish God’s kingdom. Not to maintain the status quo, but to transform the world according to divine justice and mercy.

Takeaway: The announcement of Jesus’s birth was inherently political and subversive, challenging the ultimate claims of earthly powers while offering a fundamentally different vision of salvation.

Deep Dive (15 Minutes)

Historical and Cultural Focus:
Competing Claims to Salvation

Full Passage: Luke 2:8-20 (NIV)
8 And there were shepherds living out in the fields nearby, keeping watch over their flocks at night. 9 An angel of the Lord appeared to them, and the glory of the Lord shone around them, and they were terrified. 10 But the angel said to them, “Do not be afraid. I bring you good news that will cause great joy for all the people. 11 Today in the town of David a Savior has been born to you; he is the Messiah, the Lord.”12 This will be a sign to you: You will find a baby wrapped in cloths and lying in a manger.” 13 Suddenly a great company of the heavenly host appeared with the angel, praising God and saying, 14 “Glory to God in the highest heaven, and on earth peace to those on whom his favor rests.”15 When the angels had left them and gone into heaven, the shepherds said to one another, “Let’s go to Bethlehem and see this thing that has happened, which the Lord has told us about.” 16 So they hurried off and found Mary and Joseph, and the baby, who was lying in the manger. 17 When they had seen him, they spread the word concerning what had been told them about this child, 18 and all who heard it were amazed at what the shepherds said to them. 19 But Mary treasured up all these things and pondered them in her heart. 20 The shepherds returned, glorifying and praising God for all the things they had heard and seen, which were just as they had been told.

The Roman Context

When Luke wrote his Gospel (likely in the 70s or 80s AD), the Roman Empire was at its height. Caesar Augustus (63 BC - 14 AD) had transformed Rome from a republic torn by civil war into a stable empire that brought unprecedented peace and prosperity to the Mediterranean world. He was widely celebrated as a divine figure who had saved the world from chaos.

Inscriptions from across the empire hailed Augustus with titles like “Savior of the world,” “Son of God,” and “Lord.” The famous Priene inscription from 9 BC declares that Augustus’s birth was “good news for the world” and that he was “a Savior who has made war to cease and who shall put everything in peaceful order.”

Against this backdrop, the angels’ announcement that a “Savior” has been born who is “the Lord” would have been heard as a direct challenge to imperial ideology. Luke is essentially claiming that the true Savior is not Caesar, but a Jewish baby born in poverty.

Jewish Messianic Expectations

For Jewish readers, the title “Messiah” (Christ in Greek) carried specific political and religious expectations. Most first-century Jews expected the Messiah to be a military and political leader who would overthrow foreign rule and restore Israel’s independence. Some expected him to purify the temple and reform religious practices. Others anticipated a cosmic figure who would usher in the end times and establish God’s eternal kingdom.

The announcement that the Messiah has been born would have electrified Jewish hopes for liberation. But the humble circumstances—a stable birth, shepherd witnesses, no royal court or military escort—would have been puzzling. How could this baby fulfill the grand expectations associated with the Messiah?

The Gentile Perspective

Luke wrote primarily for Gentile (non-Jewish) audiences, and his Gospel emphasizes that Jesus’s salvation extends to all people, not just Jews. For Gentile readers, the concept of a “Savior” would have been familiar from both Roman imperial propaganda and various mystery religions that promised salvation from fate, death, or spiritual bondage.

However, the kind of salvation offered by Jesus was fundamentally different from these alternatives. Roman salvation was political and social—peace through superior military power and efficient administration. Mystery religions offered individual spiritual experiences but had little to say about social justice or historical transformation.

The angels’ announcement promised something unprecedented: a salvation that was both spiritual and social, both individual and cosmic, both immediate and eternal.

The Subversive Nature of the Message

Several elements of Luke’s birth narrative would have been understood as challenges to Roman authority:

Alternative Lordship: Calling Jesus “Lord” (Kyrios in Greek) was the same title used for Caesar. Early Christians were often persecuted not for their private religious beliefs, but for refusing to acknowledge Caesar as ultimate lord.

Different Kind of Peace: The angels’ promise of “peace on earth” offered an alternative to the Pax Romana. Roman peace was maintained through military dominance and the threat of violence. The peace announced by the angels would be based on justice, mercy, and the restoration of right relationships.

Upside-Down Values: The choice of shepherds as the first witnesses embodied a reversal of social values. In a hierarchical society where status and honor were paramount, God chose to reveal the most important news in history to people at the bottom of the social ladder.

Universal Scope: The message was “for all the people,” not just for Romans or Jews. This challenged both Roman assumptions about cultural superiority and Jewish assumptions about ethnic privilege.

The Risk of Belief

For Luke’s original audience, believing and proclaiming this message involved real risk. Christians could face social ostracism, economic persecution, and even death for refusing to participate in emperor worship or for proclaiming an alternative lord.

The fact that the early Christian movement grew rapidly despite these risks suggests that people found the message compelling enough to accept significant personal cost. They believed that the salvation offered through Jesus was worth more than the security provided by conformity to imperial expectations.

The Appeal to the Marginalized

Luke’s Gospel consistently emphasizes God’s concern for the poor, the outcast, and the powerless. The choice of shepherds as the first witnesses fits this pattern. Shepherds were necessary for the economy but were often looked down upon by urban elites. They were frequently away from home, making it difficult to observe religious rituals, and their work brought them into contact with animals in ways that could make them ritually unclean.

By choosing shepherds as the first witnesses to the Messiah’s birth, God was making a statement about whose testimony matters and who is included in the kingdom. This would have been both encouraging to marginalized people and challenging to those who assumed that God’s favor followed conventional lines of social status.

Optional Journal Reflection

  1. How do you think you would have responded to the angels’ announcement if you had been living under Roman rule? Would the political implications have made you more or less likely to believe?

  2. The early Christians risked persecution for proclaiming Jesus as Lord instead of Caesar. What does this suggest about the strength of their conviction? What would it take for you to risk social or economic consequences for your beliefs?

  3. Luke emphasizes that God chose shepherds—marginalized people—as the first witnesses. What does this suggest about God’s values and priorities? How does this challenge or confirm your understanding of how God works in the world?
