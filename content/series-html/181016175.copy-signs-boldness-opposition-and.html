Day 5: Acts 5 – Joy on the Other Side of Obedience

“The apostles left the Sanhedrin, rejoicing because they had been counted worthy of suffering disgrace for the Name.” – Acts 5:41 (NIV)

Quickstart (1–3 Minute Commitment)

Today’s Passage:
Acts 5:17–42 – The apostles are arrested again, miraculously freed, threatened again, beaten, and yet continue proclaiming Jesus every day.

Greek Word:
χαίρω (chairō) – “to rejoice, to be glad.”
Not surface happiness — but a deep, Spirit-born joy that flows not from circumstances but from faithfulness.

In Acts 5, joy isn’t after relief.
It is after obedience.

What’s Happening in Acts 5:17–42?

The apostles are preaching openly, healing the sick, and gathering crowds.
The religious leaders respond with jealousy and arrest them.
But in the night, an angel opens the prison doors and sends them back into the Temple.

They return at dawn — to the exact place they were arrested — and start preaching again.

When the Sanhedrin confronts them, Peter gives one of the clearest declarations in Scripture:

“We must obey God rather than human beings.”

This isn’t rebellion for rebellion’s sake.
It is obedience to the higher authority of Jesus.

The council beats them, threatens them, and releases them.

And the apostles leave rejoicing, not because pain is good, but because they were living in the way of their crucified and risen Lord.

Acts ends this section with a remarkable summary:

“Day after day… they never stopped teaching and proclaiming the good news that Jesus is the Messiah.”
Pressure didn’t silence them.
It multiplied them.

Reflection Question

Where am I waiting for conditions to be safer or easier before I obey Jesus?

Urgency Moment

Choose one act of obedience today that you’ve been delaying.

Name it.
Do it.
Then ask the Spirit for joy on the other side of obedience.

Prayer

“Lord, give me courage to obey You even when it costs something. Form in me the joy that only comes from faithfulness to You. Teach me to say with my life, ‘I must obey God rather than human beings.’ Fill me with Your Spirit so that I walk in obedience with gladness. Amen.”

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Part 5 of 5: Joy After the Cost

By the late 1820s, after decades of labor, Wilberforce’s health was failing.
He had endured public ridicule, political isolation, smear campaigns, and countless defeats.
But he had also seen the impossible become possible — Britain had abolished the slave trade in 1807, the first major crack in an ancient system.

Yet he longed for the full abolition of slavery itself across the empire.

In 1833, as Parliament debated the final abolition bill, Wilberforce was bedridden.
Members of the Clapham community visited him daily, reading Scripture and praying with him — the same way they had prayed years earlier when the first bill failed.
Even near death, Wilberforce radiated peace. One friend recorded:

“He had joy because he felt he had obeyed God.”

On July 26, 1833, Parliament passed the Slavery Abolition Act.
Three days later, Wilberforce died.

He never walked into the House again.
He never delivered another speech.
He never saw the long-term fruit of the abolition movement.

But he died with joy.
Not because life was easy — but because he had lived faithfully.

The Clapham community later wrote that what sustained him was not success but obedience — the same joy we see in Acts 5, the joy of those who count suffering, sacrifice, and perseverance as part of following Jesus.

Takeaway:
Joy does not come from the ease of obedience, but from the faithfulness of obedience.

Dive Further (5 Minutes)

Acts 5 shows the contours of real discipleship:

  1. Obedience brings opposition.
    Faithfulness will collide with earthly powers.

  2. Opposition becomes testimony.
    The apostles’ courage is itself a witness to Jesus’ authority.

  3. The Spirit gives endurance.
    The joy in Acts 5 is not psychological — it is supernatural.

  4. Obedience turns fear into formation.
    Every cost paid for Christ becomes a place where Christ shapes the soul.
