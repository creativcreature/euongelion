How does this birth in Bethlehem connect to thousands of years of promises, prophecies, and human longing?

Yesterday we examined the dramatic announcement of Jesus’s birth. Today we’re investigating the deeper question: How does this event fit into the larger story? Luke doesn’t present Jesus’s birth as an isolated miracle, but as the culmination of a divine plan that has been unfolding for millennia. If that’s true, it changes everything about how we understand this story.

Day 2: What’s the Connection?

“Glory to God in the highest heaven, and on earth peace to those on whom his favor rests.” - Luke 2:14

Quickstart (1 Minute Commitment)

Today’s Verse: Luke 2:14 - “Glory to God in the highest heaven, and on earth peace to those on whom his favor rests.”

Hebrew Concept: שָׁלוֹם (Shalom) - Often translated as “peace,” but it means much more than the absence of conflict. It refers to wholeness, completeness, harmony, and the restoration of right relationships between God, humanity, and creation.

Reflection Question: What would it look like for there to be true “peace on earth”? What would need to change for that to become reality?

Prayer: “Help me to understand how Jesus’s birth connects to humanity’s deepest longings for peace, justice, and restoration.”

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Imagine you’ve been working on a massive jigsaw puzzle for months—one of those 5,000-piece puzzles with an intricate, beautiful image. You’ve made steady progress, connecting sections here and there, but much of it still looks like scattered pieces without clear meaning. Then one day, you find a crucial piece that suddenly makes a large section of the puzzle click into place. Suddenly, patterns that were unclear become obvious, and you can see how dozens of other pieces fit together.

This is something like what happened when Jesus was born. For centuries, God’s people had been collecting “pieces” of divine revelation—promises to Abraham about blessing all nations, prophecies about a coming king from David’s line, predictions about a suffering servant who would bear the sins of many, visions of a kingdom where justice and peace would reign forever.

Many of these pieces seemed disconnected or even contradictory. How could the Messiah be both a conquering king and a suffering servant? How could God’s kingdom be both for Israel and for all nations? How could divine justice be satisfied while divine mercy was extended to sinners?

The birth of Jesus was like finding that crucial puzzle piece that suddenly made the whole picture clear. The angels’ announcement to the shepherds wasn’t just about one baby—it was about the moment when all of God’s promises began to converge into a coherent plan for human redemption.

Takeaway: Jesus’s birth represents the moment when God’s scattered promises throughout history began to come together into a unified plan for human salvation.

Deep Dive (15 Minutes)

Old Testament Connection

The angels’ song in Luke 2:14 echoes themes that run throughout the Hebrew Scriptures. The phrase “Glory to God in the highest” connects to the temple worship described in the Psalms, where God’s glory is celebrated and proclaimed. The promise of “peace on earth” fulfills prophecies like Isaiah 9:6, which speaks of the coming child who will be called “Prince of Peace.”

The specific mention of God’s “favor” resting on people connects to the covenant promises throughout the Old Testament—God choosing to bless humanity not because of their merit, but because of His grace. This theme runs from God’s promise to Abraham (Genesis 12:1-3) through the Davidic covenant (2 Samuel 7:12-16) to the prophetic visions of restoration (Jeremiah 31:31-34).

Luke is showing that Jesus’s birth isn’t a new plan but the fulfillment of the plan God has been working out since the beginning of human history.

Theological and Historical Focus:
The Convergence of Divine Promises

Full Passage: Luke 2:8-20 (NIV)
8 And there were shepherds living out in the fields nearby, keeping watch over their flocks at night. 9 An angel of the Lord appeared to them, and the glory of the Lord shone around them, and they were terrified. 10 But the angel said to them, “Do not be afraid. I bring you good news that will cause great joy for all the people. 11 Today in the town of David a Savior has been born to you; he is the Messiah, the Lord.”12 This will be a sign to you: You will find a baby wrapped in cloths and lying in a manger.” 13 Suddenly a great company of the heavenly host appeared with the angel, praising God and saying, 14 “Glory to God in the highest heaven, and on earth peace to those on whom his favor rests.”15 When the angels had left them and gone into heaven, the shepherds said to one another, “Let’s go to Bethlehem and see this thing that has happened, which the Lord has told us about.” 16 So they hurried off and found Mary and Joseph, and the baby, who was lying in the manger. 17 When they had seen him, they spread the word concerning what had been told them about this child, 18 and all who heard it were amazed at what the shepherds said to them. 19 But Mary treasured up all these things and pondered them in her heart. 20 The shepherds returned, glorifying and praising God for all the things they had heard and seen, which were just as they had been told.

The Abrahamic Promise

The story begins with God’s promise to Abraham around 2000 BC: “All peoples on earth will be blessed through you” (Genesis 12:3). This was an extraordinary claim—that through one man’s descendants, God would somehow bring blessing to every nation and people group on earth. For centuries, this promise seemed impossible to fulfill. How could one small nation bless the entire world?

The angels’ announcement that this birth brings “good news that will cause great joy for all the people” suggests that Jesus’s birth is the beginning of the fulfillment of this ancient promise. The Savior born in Bethlehem will somehow be the means by which God’s blessing reaches every corner of the earth.

The Davidic Covenant

Around 1000 BC, God promised King David that his throne would be established forever (2 Samuel 7:12-16). This seemed to be fulfilled through David’s son Solomon, but Solomon’s kingdom eventually divided and was conquered. For centuries, the promise appeared broken. Where was David’s eternal kingdom?

The angel’s announcement that the Savior is born “in the town of David” and is “the Messiah” (literally, “the anointed one”—a royal title) suggests that Jesus is the ultimate fulfillment of the Davidic promise. His kingdom will be the eternal one that God promised to David.

The Prophetic Vision

The Hebrew prophets painted a picture of a coming age when God would establish justice, peace, and righteousness on earth. Isaiah spoke of a time when “the wolf will live with the lamb” and “they will neither harm nor destroy on all my holy mountain” (Isaiah 11:6-9). Jeremiah promised a “new covenant” where God would write His law on people’s hearts (Jeremiah 31:31-34).

The angels’ song of “peace on earth” suggests that Jesus’s birth inaugurates this prophetic vision. The peace they announce isn’t just the absence of war, but the restoration of right relationships between God and humanity, and among human beings themselves.

The Suffering Servant

One of the most puzzling elements of Old Testament prophecy was the figure of the “suffering servant” in Isaiah 53—someone who would “bear the sins of many” and through whose wounds people would be healed. How could this figure be reconciled with the victorious Messiah who would establish God’s kingdom?

The humble circumstances of Jesus’s birth—born in a stable, announced to shepherds, laid in a manger—hint at the resolution of this puzzle. The Messiah’s path to victory will lead through suffering and humility, not conventional power and glory.

The Universal Scope

Throughout the Old Testament, there’s a tension between God’s special relationship with Israel and His concern for all nations. The prophets spoke of a time when all peoples would come to worship the God of Israel, but how would this happen?

The angels’ announcement that this is “good news for all the people” suggests that Jesus’s birth begins the resolution of this tension. Through the Jewish Messiah, God’s salvation will extend to every nation and people group.

The Historical Timing

Luke is careful to place Jesus’s birth in specific historical context—during the reign of Caesar Augustus, when Quirinius was governor of Syria (Luke 2:1-2). This isn’t just historical detail; it’s theological statement. The birth of the true King occurs during the reign of the most powerful earthly ruler of the time, suggesting that God’s kingdom operates by different principles than human kingdoms.

Optional Journal Reflection

  1. How does understanding Jesus’s birth as the fulfillment of ancient promises change your perspective on the story? Does it make the claims more or less credible to you?

  2. The angels announced “peace on earth,” but the world still seems full of conflict and suffering. How do you reconcile this promise with current reality?

  3. What does it mean that God’s plan for human salvation took thousands of years to unfold? What does this suggest about God’s character and methods?
