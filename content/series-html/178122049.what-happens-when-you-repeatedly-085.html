What practical steps can I take to break cycles of repeated sin and walk in the Spirit’s power?

Understanding grace, forgiveness, and God’s power working through weakness is crucial, but it must lead to practical action. Today we examine Paul’s most comprehensive teaching on how believers can actually experience victory over persistent sinful patterns. The key isn’t trying harder through willpower but learning to walk in dependence on the Holy Spirit’s power. This passage provides a roadmap for moving from repeated failure to gradual transformation.

Day 5 of 6: What Will I Do?

“So I say, walk by the Spirit, and you will not gratify the desires of the flesh.” - Galatians 5:16

Quickstart (1 Minute Commitment)

Today’s Verse: Galatians 5:16 - “So I say, walk by the Spirit, and you will not gratify the desires of the flesh.”

Greek Word: περιπατέω (Peripateo) - “To walk” or “to conduct one’s life.” This isn’t about physical walking but about the ongoing conduct of daily life—how we make decisions, respond to temptation, and live out our faith.

Reflection Question: What would it look like practically to “walk by the Spirit” in the specific area where I struggle most with repeated sin?

Urgency Moment: Stop trying to overcome persistent sin through willpower alone. Identify one specific way you can depend on the Holy Spirit’s power today instead of your own strength.

Prayer: “Teach me what it means to walk by your Spirit and depend on your power rather than my own efforts.”

Story Time (5 Minutes)

One year after his journey toward recovery began, Marcus Thompson faced his biggest test yet. His company was going through layoffs, his teenage daughter had been diagnosed with a chronic illness, and his mother-in-law had moved in with them after a stroke. The stress was overwhelming, and every fiber of his being wanted to escape to the casino where he could forget his responsibilities for a few hours.

But instead of driving to the casino, Marcus found himself sitting in his car in the church parking lot, calling his pastor. “Pastor Williams, I’m about to make a really bad decision. I can feel it. The urge to gamble is stronger than it’s been in months, and I don’t think I can resist it on my own.”

“Marcus, I’m proud of you for calling instead of just going,” Pastor Williams replied. “Tell me what you’ve learned about walking in the Spirit when you’re facing temptation.”

Marcus took a deep breath. “Well, I’ve learned that trying to fight the urge through willpower doesn’t work. The harder I try not to think about gambling, the more I think about it. But when I focus on depending on God’s Spirit instead of my own strength, something different happens.”

“What do you mean?”

“Instead of trying to resist the temptation, I’ve learned to do something positive that requires dependence on God. Like right now, instead of sitting here fighting the urge to gamble, I’m going to go visit Mrs. Henderson from our church who’s been in the hospital. I’ll pray for her, encourage her, and serve someone else instead of focusing on my own escape.”

Pastor Williams smiled, even though Marcus couldn’t see it through the phone. “That’s exactly what Paul means by walking in the Spirit. You’re not just avoiding sin—you’re actively choosing to live in dependence on God’s power for something positive.”

As Marcus drove to the hospital instead of the casino, he reflected on how his approach to temptation had changed over the past year. He had stopped trying to be strong enough to resist sin and had started learning to be dependent enough to choose righteousness. The difference was subtle but transformative.

At the hospital, as he prayed with Mrs. Henderson and listened to her share about her fears and hopes, Marcus realized that the urge to gamble had completely disappeared. He wasn’t fighting it anymore—he was simply living in a different reality, one where his focus was on God’s purposes rather than his own escape.

When he got home that evening, his wife asked how his day had gone. “It was hard,” Marcus replied honestly. “I wanted to gamble more than I have in months. But instead of trying to be strong enough to resist, I chose to be dependent enough to serve. And God’s Spirit gave me exactly what I needed.”

Takeaway: Walking in the Spirit isn’t about being strong enough to resist sin but about being dependent enough to choose righteousness through God’s power.

Deep Dive (15 Minutes)

Practical Application Focus:
Learning to Walk in the Spirit’s Power

Full Passage: Galatians 5:16-25 (NIV)
16 So I say, walk by the Spirit, and you will not gratify the desires of the flesh. 17 For the flesh desires what is contrary to the Spirit, and the Spirit what is contrary to the flesh. They are in conflict with each other, so that you are not to do whatever you want. 18 But if you are led by the Spirit, you are not under the law.19 The acts of the flesh are obvious: sexual immorality, impurity and debauchery; 20 idolatry and witchcraft; hatred, discord, jealousy, fits of rage, selfish ambition, dissensions, factions 21 and envy; drunkenness, orgies, and the like. I warn you, as I did before, that those who live like this will not inherit the kingdom of God.22 But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, forbearance, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, 23 gentleness and self-control. Against such things there is no law. 24 Those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires. 25 Since we live by the Spirit, let us keep in step with the Spirit.

The Context of Spiritual Warfare

Paul’s instruction to “walk by the Spirit” comes in the context of his discussion about Christian freedom and the ongoing conflict between the Spirit and the flesh in the believer’s life. This isn’t a theoretical discussion but practical guidance for how to live victoriously in the midst of real spiritual warfare.

The Galatian believers were struggling with legalism on one side (trying to earn God’s favor through rule-keeping) and license on the other side (using freedom as an excuse for sinful behavior). Paul’s solution is neither more rules nor more freedom but dependence on the Holy Spirit’s power.

The Nature of Walking

The Greek word περιπατέω (peripateo) literally means “to walk around” but metaphorically refers to one’s entire way of life. Walking implies several important characteristics:

Continuous Action: Walking is not a one-time decision but an ongoing series of steps taken consistently over time.

Directional Movement: Walking involves moving toward a destination rather than wandering aimlessly.

Dependent Rhythm: Walking requires constant attention and adjustment to maintain balance and direction.

Progressive Development: Walking skills improve with practice and experience over time.

The Promise and the Process

Paul’s statement contains both a promise and a process:

The Promise: “You will not gratify the desires of the flesh” (v. 16). This is not a suggestion but a definitive statement about what happens when believers walk by the Spirit.

The Process: Walking by the Spirit is an ongoing discipline that requires conscious choice and dependence on God’s power rather than human willpower.

The promise is conditional on the process—victory over fleshly desires comes through walking in the Spirit, not through trying harder to resist temptation.

The Conflict Between Flesh and Spirit

Paul describes an ongoing conflict between two opposing forces in the believer’s life:

The Flesh (σάρξ): This refers not to the physical body but to the fallen nature that remains in believers—the part of us that is still influenced by sin and self-centeredness.

The Spirit (πνεῦμα): This refers to the Holy Spirit who dwells in believers and produces godly desires and the power to fulfill them.

This conflict explains why believers experience internal struggle and why victory requires dependence on the Spirit rather than human effort alone.

Old Testament Connection

Paul’s concept of walking with God has deep Old Testament roots. Enoch “walked faithfully with God” (Genesis 5:24), and Noah was described as one who “walked faithfully with God” (Genesis 6:9). The prophet Micah summarized God’s requirements as “to act justly and to love mercy and to walk humbly with your God” (Micah 6:8).

The Old Testament also contains the promise of a new covenant where God would put his Spirit within his people to enable them to follow his decrees: “I will give you a new heart and put a new spirit in you; I will remove from you your heart of stone and give you a heart of flesh. And I will put my Spirit in you and move you to follow my decrees and be careful to keep my laws” (Ezekiel 36:26-27). Paul’s teaching about walking in the Spirit is the fulfillment of this Old Testament promise.

The Works of the Flesh

Paul provides a comprehensive list of behaviors that characterize life according to the flesh (vv. 19-21). These can be categorized into several areas:

Sexual Sins: Sexual immorality, impurity, debauchery
Religious Sins: Idolatry, witchcraft
Social Sins: Hatred, discord, jealousy, fits of rage, selfish ambition, dissensions, factions, envy Substance Abuse: Drunkenness, orgies

Paul’s phrase “and the like” indicates that this list is representative rather than exhaustive. The common thread is self-centeredness and the pursuit of immediate gratification without regard for God’s standards or others’ welfare.

The Fruit of the Spirit

In contrast to the works of the flesh, Paul describes the fruit of the Spirit (vv. 22-23). This fruit includes:

Love (ἀγάπη): Self-sacrificial care for others’ welfare
Joy (χαρά): Deep contentment that doesn’t depend on circumstances
Peace (εἰρήνη): Inner tranquility and harmony with God and others
Forbearance (μακροθυμία): Patient endurance in difficult relationships
Kindness (χρηστότης): Gentle, considerate treatment of others
Goodness (ἀγαθωσύνη): Active benevolence and generosity
Faithfulness (πίστις): Reliability and trustworthiness
Gentleness (πραΰτης): Strength under control, humility
Self-control (ἐγκράτεια): Mastery over one’s desires and impulses

The Difference Between Works and Fruit

Paul’s choice of words is significant:

Works of the Flesh: These are things we do through human effort and self-will. They require no special power or transformation—they come naturally to fallen human nature.

Fruit of the Spirit: These are things that grow in us through the Spirit’s power. Like fruit on a tree, they develop naturally when we’re connected to the right source of life and nourishment.

This distinction is crucial for understanding how transformation actually happens. We don’t produce spiritual fruit through effort but through dependence on the Spirit who produces it in us.

Practical Steps for Walking in the Spirit

While Paul doesn’t provide a detailed methodology, several practical principles emerge from his teaching:

Focus on Dependence, Not Resistance: Instead of trying to resist fleshly desires through willpower, focus on depending on the Spirit for power to choose righteousness.

Cultivate Spiritual Disciplines: Regular prayer, Bible study, worship, and fellowship create conditions where the Spirit can work more freely in our lives.

Choose Positive Actions: Instead of just avoiding sin, actively choose behaviors that require dependence on God’s power and serve his purposes.

Seek Community Support: Walking in the Spirit is easier in the context of relationships with other believers who can provide encouragement and accountability.

Practice Moment-by-Moment Dependence: Walking in the Spirit isn’t a one-time decision but a series of moment-by-moment choices to depend on God’s power rather than our own.

The Role of the Law

Paul explains that those who are led by the Spirit are “not under the law” (v. 18). This doesn’t mean they’re free to sin but that they have a different relationship to God’s standards:

Under Law: Trying to earn God’s favor through perfect obedience, which leads to either pride (if we think we’re succeeding) or despair (when we realize we’re failing).

Led by Spirit: Receiving God’s favor as a gift and being empowered by his Spirit to live in ways that please him, which leads to grateful obedience and gradual transformation.

The Reality of Crucifixion

Paul reminds his readers that “those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires” (v. 24). This crucifixion is:

Already Accomplished: It happened definitively at conversion, not gradually over time.

Personally Applied: Each believer has participated in this crucifixion through union with Christ.

Practically Lived: The reality of crucifixion must be lived out through daily choices to depend on the Spirit rather than the flesh.

Keeping in Step with the Spirit

Paul concludes with the exhortation to “keep in step with the Spirit” (v. 25). The Greek word στοιχέω (stoicheo) means “to walk in line” or “to march in formation.” This suggests:

Alignment: Our lives should be aligned with the Spirit’s direction and purposes.

Rhythm: We should move at the Spirit’s pace rather than rushing ahead or lagging behind.

Coordination: Our actions should be coordinated with the Spirit’s work rather than working against it.

Consistency: We should maintain this alignment consistently rather than sporadically.

Dealing with Repeated Failures

Paul’s teaching provides several insights for believers who struggle with repeated failures in the same areas:

Expect Conflict: The ongoing conflict between flesh and Spirit means that struggle is normal, not evidence of false faith.

Focus on Process: Victory comes through learning to walk in the Spirit consistently, not through achieving sinless perfection.

Seek Transformation: The goal isn’t just behavior modification but the development of spiritual fruit that naturally crowds out fleshly works.

Practice Dependence: Each failure can become an opportunity to learn greater dependence on the Spirit’s power rather than human willpower.

The Community Aspect

Walking in the Spirit isn’t meant to be a solitary endeavor. The context of Galatians suggests several community aspects:

Mutual Support: Believers should help each other learn to walk in the Spirit rather than judging each other’s failures.

Shared Accountability: Community can provide gentle accountability that helps us recognize when we’re walking in the flesh rather than the Spirit.

Corporate Worship: Gathering with other believers for worship, prayer, and Bible study creates an environment where the Spirit can work more freely.

Service Opportunities: Serving others provides concrete ways to practice walking in the Spirit and developing spiritual fruit.

The Long-term Perspective

Paul’s teaching emphasizes that walking in the Spirit is a lifelong process rather than a quick fix:

Gradual Development: Spiritual fruit develops over time, like fruit on a tree, rather than appearing instantly.

Increasing Maturity: As we learn to walk in the Spirit more consistently, we experience greater freedom from fleshly patterns.

Ongoing Dependence: Even mature believers need to continue depending on the Spirit’s power rather than becoming self-sufficient.

Ultimate Victory: While the struggle continues in this life, ultimate victory over sin is assured through Christ.

Optional Journal Reflection

  1. What does “walking in the Spirit” look like practically in your daily life? What specific steps can you take to depend more on the Spirit’s power and less on your own willpower?

  2. Which works of the flesh do you struggle with most? Which fruit of the Spirit do you most need to see developed in your life? How might these be connected?

  3. How can you better utilize Christian community to support your efforts to walk in the Spirit? What role should other believers play in your process of transformation?

Share Your Thoughts

What does it mean to you that victory comes through dependence on the Spirit rather than through human willpower? Share your thoughts on practical ways to cultivate this dependence in daily life.

Greek Language Learning: Week 4, Day 5

The Language of Walking in the Spirit

Today we’re learning vocabulary related to spiritual walking, the conflict between flesh and Spirit, and the fruit that grows from dependence on God’s power.

Walking and Conduct Vocabulary

περιπατέω (peripateo) - “I walk” or “I conduct my life”
πορεύομαι (poreuomai) - “I go” or “I journey”
στοιχέω (stoicheo) - “I walk in line” or “I keep in step”

περιπατεῖτε πνεύματι
(peripateite pneumati) -
“walk by the Spirit”

Flesh and Spirit Language

σάρξ (sarx) - “flesh” (fallen nature)
πνεῦμα (pneuma) - “Spirit”
σαρκικός (sarkikos) - “fleshly” or “carnal”
πνευματικός (pneumatikos) - “spiritual”

The Language of Desire and Conflict

ἐπιθυμία (epithymia) - “desire” or “lust”
ἐπιθυμέω (epithymeo) - “I desire” or “I lust after”

ἡ σὰρξ ἐπιθυμεῖ κατὰ τοῦ πνεύματος
(he sarx epithymei kata tou pneumatos)
“the flesh desires against the Spirit”

Conflict and Opposition

ἀντίκειμαι (antikeimai) - “I oppose” or “I am contrary to”
μάχομαι (machomai) - “I fight” or “I struggle”

ταῦτα γὰρ ἀλλήλοις ἀντίκειται
(tauta gar allelois antikeitai)
“for these are opposed to one another”

Works and Fruit Language

ἔργον (ergon) - “work” or “deed”
καρπός (karpos) - “fruit”

τὰ ἔργα τῆς σαρκός
(ta erga tes sarkos) -
“the works of the flesh”

ὁ καρπὸς τοῦ πνεύματος (
ho karpos tou pneumatos) -
“the fruit of the Spirit”

The Fruit of the Spirit

ἀγάπη (agape) - “love”
χαρά (chara) - “joy”
εἰρήνη (eirene) - “peace”
μακροθυμία (makrothymia) - “patience/forbearance”
χρηστότης (chrestotes) - “kindness”
ἀγαθωσύνη (agathosyne) - “goodness”
πίστις (pistis) - “faithfulness”
πραΰτης (prautes) - “gentleness”
ἐγκράτεια (egkrateia) - “self-control”

Leading and Guidance Language

ἄγω (ago) - “I lead”
ὁδηγέω (odegeo) - “I guide”

εἰ δὲ πνεύματι ἄγεσθε
(ei de pneumati agesthe)
“but if you are led by the Spirit”

Crucifixion Language

σταυρόω (stauroo) - “I crucify”
σταυρός (stauros) - “cross”

οἱ δὲ τοῦ Χριστοῦ τὴν σάρκα ἐσταύρωσαν
(hoi de tou Christou ten sarka estaurosan)
“those who belong to Christ have crucified the flesh”

Living and Life Language

ζάω (zao) - “I live”
ζωή (zoe) - “life”

εἰ ζῶμεν πνεύματι
(ei zomen pneumati) -
“if we live by the Spirit”

Reading Practice

λέγω δέ, πνεύματι περιπατεῖτε καὶ ἐπιθυμίαν σαρκὸς οὐ μὴ τελέσητε
(lego de, pneumati peripateite kai epithymian sarkos ou me telesete)
“But I say, walk by the Spirit, and you will not carry out the desire of the flesh”

The Grammar of Spiritual Conflict

Present Imperatives: περιπατεῖτε (peripateite) - “keep walking” (continuous action)
Double Negative: οὐ μή (ou me) - strongest possible negative in Greek
Subjunctive Mood: τελέσητε (telesete) - “you might carry out” (possibility denied)

Conditional Sentences

εἰ δὲ πνεύματι ἄγεσθε, οὐκ ἐστὲ ὑπὸ νόμον
(ei de pneumati agesthe, ouk este hypo nomon)
“but if you are led by the Spirit, you are not under law”

Memory Tip

στοιχέω (stoicheo) gives us English “stoichiometry” - keeping elements in proper proportion, like keeping in step with the Spirit!

Practice Exercise

  1. ἔργα τῆς σαρκός = ?

Answers: 1. “walk by the Spirit,” 2. “fruit of the Spirit,” 3. “works of the flesh”
