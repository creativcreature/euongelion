This is Day 2 of “The Work of God” Catching up? [Start Here]

What is the difference between the temporary satisfaction the world offers and the true, life-giving sustenance that comes only from God?

Yesterday, Jesus challenged the crowd to seek the food that endures to eternal life, defining the “work of God” not as religious performance but as belief in him. Today, the crowd pushes back. They demand a sign, comparing Jesus to Moses, who gave their ancestors manna in the wilderness. Jesus uses this challenge to make a profound distinction between the temporary, physical bread of the past and the true, spiritual bread that the Father is offering them in the present—a bread that gives life not just to a single nation, but to the entire world.

Greek Word: ἀληθινός (alēthinos) - “true,” “real,” “genuine.” This word means more than just “not false.” It signifies the ultimate reality of which all other things are just a shadow or a copy. The manna was a real sign, but Jesus is the true bread.

Reflection Question: What temporary “manna” am I relying on for daily satisfaction (e.g., social media likes, daily accomplishments, financial security)? How is it failing to provide true, lasting life?

Story Time (5 Minutes)

Liam sat at the long Thanksgiving table, listening to the familiar rhythm of family stories. His brother was recounting a recent trip to Europe, describing the fine meals and luxurious hotels. His cousin was talking about her son’s acceptance into a prestigious university. It was the same as every year—a parade of achievements, a feast of accomplishments.

In the past, this would have filled Liam with a mixture of envy and inadequacy. He would have been mentally preparing his own meager offering to this potluck of success. But today, something was different. After his moment of clarity in the study the day before, he was hearing the conversation with new ears.

He realized that all these stories, as impressive as they were, were stories about “manna.” They were about temporary provision, fleeting successes, food that spoils. The European vacation was over. The university acceptance was a great achievement, but it was not a guarantee of a happy or meaningful life. It was all bread for a day.

He looked across the table at his father, a man who had built a successful business from nothing. For his entire life, his father had been a provider of manna—for his family, for his employees, for his community. Liam had always seen this as the ultimate success. But now, he saw the toll it had taken. His father looked tired, burdened by the constant pressure to keep the manna coming.

Later, as Liam was helping his mother clear the table, she spoke quietly to him. “Your father is proud of you, you know.”

“He has a funny way of showing it,” Liam replied, stacking the plates.

“He doesn’t know how to show it,” she corrected gently. “All he knows is how to provide. He thinks that’s what love is. He gave you and your brother everything he never had. But I think he’s starting to realize it wasn’t enough.”

That evening, Liam found his father sitting alone on the porch, staring out at the darkening sky. Liam sat down next to him, and for a long time, they just sat in silence.

Finally, Liam spoke. “Dad, I’ve been thinking about the story of the manna in the wilderness. It was a miracle, a gift from God to keep the people alive. But it was never meant to be the final destination. It was just provision for the journey.”

His father looked at him, a flicker of interest in his tired eyes.

“I feel like we spend our whole lives chasing after manna,” Liam continued, his voice quiet but sure. “The next promotion, the next success, the next achievement. We think that’s the bread that will satisfy us. But it always runs out. Jesus said that the true bread, the bread that gives real life, is a gift from the Father. It’s not something we can earn or achieve.”

His father didn’t respond, but he didn’t look away. He just kept looking at his son, as if seeing him for the first time.

Liam wasn’t trying to preach. He was simply sharing the truth that was beginning to transform his own heart. He was realizing that the hunger he felt, the hunger they all felt, was not for more manna. It was for the true bread from heaven. And for the first time, he felt a deep, genuine gratitude, not for what he had achieved, but for the gift he was beginning to receive.

Takeaway: The world offers us an endless supply of temporary “manna”—achievements, possessions, and experiences that provide fleeting satisfaction. God offers us the “true bread”—a life-giving relationship with his Son that satisfies our deepest spiritual hunger.

Deep Dive (15 Minutes)

Practical and Theological Focus:
Manna as a Shadow, Christ as the Reality

Full Passage: John 6:25-40 (NIV)
25 When they found him on the other side of the lake, they asked him, “Rabbi, when did you get here?” 26 Jesus answered, “Very truly I tell you, you are looking for me, not because you saw the signs I performed but because you ate the loaves and had your fill. 27 Do not work for food that spoils, but for food that endures to eternal life, which the Son of Man will give you. For on him God the Father has placed his seal of approval.” 28 Then they asked him, “What must we do to do the works God requires?” 29 Jesus answered, “The work of God is this: to believe in the one he has sent.” 30 So they asked him, “What sign then will you give that we may see it and believe you? What will you do? 31 Our ancestors ate the manna in the wilderness; as it is written: ‘He gave them bread from heaven to eat.’” 32 Jesus said to them, “Very truly I tell you, it is not Moses who has given you the bread from heaven, but it is my Father who gives you the true bread from heaven. 33 For the bread of God is the bread that comes down from heaven and gives life to the world.” 34 “Sir,” they said, “always give us this bread.” 35 Then Jesus declared, “I am the bread of life. Whoever comes to me will never go hungry, and whoever believes in me will never be thirsty. 36 But as I told you, you have seen me and still you do not believe. 37 All those the Father gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me I will never drive away. 38 For I have come down from heaven not to do my will but to do the will of him who sent me. 39 And this is the will of him who sent me, that I shall lose none of all those he has given me, but raise them up at the last day. 40 For my Father’s will is that everyone who looks to the Son and believes in him shall have eternal life, and I will raise them up at the last day.”

The Demand for a Sign

The crowd’s response to Jesus’s call to believe is telling: they demand a sign. “What will you do?” they ask. They then bring up the ultimate historical precedent: the manna in the wilderness. Their logic is simple: “Moses gave our ancestors bread from heaven. If you want us to believe in you, you need to top that.”

They are still thinking in transactional terms. They are looking for a miracle worker, a provider of physical bread, not a savior of souls.

Jesus’s Correction

Jesus makes two crucial corrections to their understanding:

  1. The Source was not Moses, but the Father: “It is not Moses who has given you the bread... but it is my Father who gives you the true bread.” Jesus shifts their focus from the human agent (Moses) to the divine source (the Father). The manna was a gift from God, not a feat of Moses.

  2. The Manna was a Shadow, not the Reality: Jesus introduces the concept of the “true bread” (τὸν ἄρτον τὸν ἀληθινόν, ton arton ton alēthinon). The word ἀληθινός means “genuine” or “real.” It implies that the manna, as miraculous as it was, was only a temporary, physical foreshadowing of a greater, spiritual reality.

The Nature of the True Bread

Jesus then describes the characteristics of this “true bread”:

• It comes from heaven: It is of divine origin, not earthly.
• It gives life to the world: Its scope is universal, not limited to the nation of Israel.

The manna sustained the life of one nation in the wilderness; the true bread gives eternal life to the entire world.

This is a radical expansion of the crowd’s vision. They are thinking about their own history and their own needs. Jesus is talking about God’s plan for the redemption of all humanity.

Old Testament Connection

The manna in the wilderness (Exodus 16) is one of the most significant events in Israel’s history. It was a daily reminder of God’s miraculous provision and their complete dependence on him. However, the Old Testament itself hints that there is a deeper hunger that physical bread cannot satisfy.

Deuteronomy 8:3: “He humbled you, causing you to hunger and then feeding you with manna, which neither you nor your ancestors had known, to teach you that man does not live on bread alone but on every word that comes from the mouth of the Lord.”

Jesus is now claiming to be the fulfillment of this verse. He is the living “word” from the mouth of the Lord, the true sustenance for the human soul.

The Crowd’s Misunderstanding

The crowd’s response shows they still don’t get it: “Sir, always give us this bread.” They are still thinking of a continuous supply of physical or miraculous food. They want the benefits of the bread without understanding its true nature or the one who provides it.

This is a common spiritual pitfall. We often want God’s gifts (peace, joy, provision) without wanting God himself. We want the bread, but not the Baker. Jesus will challenge this superficial desire in the verses that follow.

Optional Journal Reflection

  1. Make a list of the “manna” you are currently pursuing in your life. How does it compare to the “true bread” Jesus describes?

  2. In what ways are you, like the crowd, asking God for his gifts while neglecting a relationship with the Giver? How can you shift your focus from the bread to the Baker?

Share Your Thoughts

Why do you think it is so easy to settle for temporary satisfaction instead of seeking the true, life-giving bread? What practical steps can we take to cultivate a deeper hunger for God himself?

Greek Language Learning: Week 8, Day 2

The Language of True and Heavenly Things

Today, we learn the Greek words that distinguish between the temporary sign (manna) and the ultimate reality (Christ).

Bread:
ἄρτος (artos) - “bread,” “loaf”

Heaven:
οὐρανός (ouranos) - “heaven,” “sky”

True and Real:
ἀληθής (alēthēs) - “true” (as in, not false)
ἀληθινός (alēthinos) - “true,” “real,” “genuine” (the ultimate reality)

Giving and Receiving:
δίδωμι (didōmi) - “I give”
λαμβάνω (lambanō) - “I receive”

Life and World:
ζωή (zōē) - “life”
κόσμος (kosmos) - “world,” “universe”

Reading Practice

ὁ γὰρ ἄρτος τοῦ θεοῦ ἐστιν ὁ καταβαίνων ἐκ τοῦ οὐρανοῦ καὶ ζωὴν διδοὺς τῷ κόσμῳ.
(ho gar artos tou theou estin ho katabainōn ek tou ouranou kai zōēn didous tō kosmō.)
“For the bread of God is he who comes down from heaven and gives life to the world.”

Grammar Note: The Present Participle

ὁ καταβαίνων (”he who comes down”) and διδοὺς (”giving”) are present participles. They describe the ongoing character and action of the “bread of God.” He is continually coming down from heaven and continually giving life. This is not a one-time event, but a continuous reality.

Memory Tip

Distinguish between ἀληθής (alēthēs) and ἀληθινός (alēthinos). A good photo is ἀληθής (a true likeness), but the person themselves is ἀληθινός (the real thing). The manna was ἀληθής (a true sign), but Jesus is ἀληθινός (the real bread).

Practice Exercise

Fill in the blanks with the correct Greek concept:

  1. The manna was a temporary bread, but Jesus is the ___ bread (ἀληθινός).

  1. The true bread comes down from ___ (οὐρανός).

  1. It gives ___ to the world (ζωή).

Answers: 1. true, 2. heaven, 3. life
