The Name You're Trying to Pronounce

You're probably wondering how to say it.

Eu-on-GEL-ee-on.

It's Greek. And it means something you've heard a thousand times but maybe never really heard: good news.

Not "good advice." Not "good vibes." Not "good luck."

Good news.

The kind of news that changes everything. The kind that makes a messenger run across a battlefield, lungs burning, legs failing, because the outcome of the war has shifted and everyone needs to know. That's what euongelion meant in the ancient world—the announcement that the battle is over, the king has won, and everything is about to change.1

This is where you've landed. Not an app. Not a program. Not another thing to add to your already overflowing life.

A path home.

Why You're Here

We don't know exactly what brought you here. Maybe someone shared a link. Maybe you were searching for something at 2 AM when the house was quiet and your thoughts weren't. Maybe you used to believe and don't anymore. Maybe you still believe but it feels like going through the motions. Maybe you've never believed but something keeps pulling at you.

It doesn't matter which door you came through. You're here now.

Not religion. Not ritual. Not rule-following.

Relationship.

The kind where you're known and loved. The kind where your questions are welcome. The kind where you don't have to pretend to have it together.

"The Lord your God is in your midst, a mighty one who will save; he will rejoice over you with gladness; he will quiet you by his love; he will exult over you with loud singing." — Zephaniah 3:17 (ESV)

That's not the distant, angry God you might have been taught to fear. That's a Father who sings over His children.

And He's been waiting for you to come home.

What This Place Is

EUONGELION is daily bread for hungry souls.

That phrase—daily bread—comes from the prayer Jesus taught His followers:

"Give us this day our daily bread." — Matthew 6:11 (ESV)

In the ancient world, bread wasn't a side dish. It was survival. It was the thing you couldn't live without. And Jesus taught us to ask for it daily—not weekly, not monthly, not in bulk. Every single day, we come with open hands.

That's what this is. A daily practice of coming to God with open hands.

Word • Thing • Event

The Hebrew Word for "Word"

The Hebrew word for "word" is davar (דָּבָר).

Pronounced: dah-VAHR.

But here's what's fascinating: davar doesn't just mean "word" the way we think of it—sounds that convey meaning. In Hebrew, davar also means "thing" or "matter" or "event."4

When God speaks, things happen.

"And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light."5

The word and the reality are the same. God's speech isn't just information—it's creation. It's power. It's reality coming into being.

This matters for how you approach Scripture. The Bible isn't just words about God. It's God's davar—His speech that creates, shapes, transforms.

When you read these devotionals, you're not just learning information. You're encountering the living Word of a God who speaks things into existence.

Including new life in you.

A Story: The Long Way Home

In 1972, a Japanese soldier named Hiroo Onoda finally emerged from the Philippine jungle.

He had been hiding for twenty-nine years.

Onoda was sent to Lubang Island in 1944 with orders to conduct guerrilla warfare and never surrender. When the war ended in 1945, he didn't believe it. Leaflets were dropped announcing Japan's surrender—he thought they were propaganda. Family members came to the jungle with loudspeakers, begging him to come out—he thought it was a trick. For nearly three decades, he lived in the mountains, convinced the war was still going on, waiting for orders that would never come.6

It wasn't until his former commanding officer traveled to the Philippines and personally issued the order to stand down that Onoda finally surrendered his sword and rifle.

He had been free for twenty-nine years. He just didn't know it.

Here's why that story matters:

Some of you have been living in a war that's already over.

You've been hiding, fighting, surviving—convinced that you have to earn God's favor, that you're not good enough, that the battle is still raging. But the announcement has already been made. The euongelion has already been declared.

"It is finished."7

Jesus said those words from the cross. It is finished. The debt is paid. The war is won. You don't have to keep fighting.

You just have to come out of the jungle.

An Invitation

Before we close this first day, let's do something together.

Find a quiet spot. If you can't find quiet, find stillness—even if the noise continues around you.

Take a breath. A real one. Feel your lungs expand. Feel them release.

Now read these words slowly, out loud if you can:

"The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want.
He makes me lie down in green pastures.
He leads me beside still waters.
He restores my soul." — Psalm 23:1-3 (ESV)

That's what's being offered here. Not a productivity hack for your spiritual life. Not another thing to optimize.

Soul restoration.

The Hebrew word for "restore" is shuv (שׁוּב)—to return, to come back, to go home.11

That's the invitation. Come home.

You don't have to have it all figured out. You don't have to clean up first. You don't have to perform or pretend.

Just come.

Interactive Element

Breath Prayer for Today

A breath prayer is a short phrase you repeat with your breathing—inhale on the first part, exhale on the second.

Inhale: "Lord, I am here."
Exhale: "Lead me home."

Try it now. Three times. Slowly.

Carry this with you today. In the car. In line at the store. When your mind wanders. When anxiety rises.

Lord, I am here. Lead me home.

Reflection Questions

  3. The Hebrew word shuv means "to return" or "come home." What does "home" mean to you spiritually? Have you ever felt at home with God?
