# All Things Work Together

## A Deep Dive into Romans 8:28

---

_Series: Deep Dive Samples_
_Meta-Story Placement: Church — The Spirit's work in the already-but-not-yet kingdom_

---

## The Scripture

> **And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose.**
>
> — Romans 8:28 (ESV)

---

## Section A: The Hook

### The Letter She Almost Didn't Open

The envelope sat on her kitchen counter for three days.

Maria knew what it was. The return address was from the adoption agency—the same agency that had placed her daughter with a family eighteen years ago. The daughter she had been seventeen, terrified, and alone when she gave up. The daughter she had never stopped thinking about.

For eighteen years, Maria had wondered. Did she make the right choice? Was her daughter happy? Did she hate the woman who gave her away? Every birthday, every graduation season, every random Wednesday when the weight of it all pressed down—she wondered.

Now an answer sat in a white envelope, and she could not bring herself to open it.

---

It was her pastor who finally asked the question she had been avoiding.

"What are you afraid you'll find?"

"That I ruined her life," Maria said. "That she's angry. That giving her up was the worst decision I ever made."

The pastor nodded. "And what if you discover it was the best decision?"

Maria had no answer.

"Here's what I've learned," the pastor continued. "Sometimes God takes our worst moments—the ones we're most ashamed of—and weaves them into something we never could have imagined. Not erased. Not undone. Woven."

---

That night, she opened the letter.

It was from her daughter. Her name was Elena, and she had been looking for Maria for two years. She had been raised by loving parents, gotten a scholarship to study medicine, and was now in her first year of residency. She wasn't angry. She wanted to meet.

At the bottom of the letter, Elena had written: "I don't know if you're a person of faith, but I want you to know something. My parents told me that the day I was placed in their arms, they prayed a prayer of thanks for the woman who chose life for me when it would have been easier not to. You gave me to them, and they gave me to God. I've never thought of you as someone who abandoned me. I've always thought of you as the first person who loved me enough to do something impossibly hard."

Maria wept for an hour.

Not tears of grief. Tears of astonishment. The decision that had haunted her for eighteen years had become a story she could not have written herself.

---

### The Verse We Misuse

Romans 8:28 is one of the most quoted—and most mishandled—verses in Scripture.

We plaster it on sympathy cards. We offer it as comfort to the grieving, often too soon. We treat it like a theological Band-Aid, slapping it over wounds that need much more careful attention.

"Well, at least we know all things work together for good!"

It can sound hollow. It can sound cruel. It can sound like we're asking people to pretend their pain isn't real because, somewhere down the line, it will all make sense.

That is not what Paul means.

Romans 8:28 is not a promise that bad things are secretly good. It is not a denial of suffering. It is not a requirement to smile through trauma because "God is in control."

It is something far more profound—and far more difficult—than any of that.

---

### Where We Are in God's Story

Romans 8 is the climax of Paul's argument about life in the Spirit. We are in the Church era of God's story—the already-but-not-yet time between Christ's resurrection and his return. The kingdom has been inaugurated but not consummated. We have the firstfruits of the Spirit but not yet the full harvest.

This is crucial context. Paul is not writing to people whose lives are easy. He is writing to a church in Rome facing potential persecution, social marginalization, and the constant tension of living as citizens of heaven in a hostile empire.¹

Just a few verses later, Paul will ask: "Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? Shall tribulation, or distress, or persecution, or famine, or nakedness, or danger, or sword?" (Romans 8:35).

These are not hypotheticals. These are the actual circumstances of the early church. And it is into this context—not a context of comfort and ease—that Paul declares: all things work together for good.

The promise is not that life will be pleasant. The promise is that nothing is wasted.

---

## Section B: The Text

### The Immediate Context

Romans 8:28 does not stand alone. It is part of a larger argument that runs from verse 18 through verse 39—a sustained meditation on suffering and glory.

Paul begins in verse 18: "I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us."

He then describes creation itself groaning, waiting for redemption (vv. 19-22). He describes believers groaning inwardly, waiting for the completion of their adoption (vv. 23-25). He describes the Spirit groaning in intercession when we do not know how to pray (vv. 26-27).

Three groanings. Creation. Believers. The Spirit.

It is in this context—a world shot through with longing and incompleteness—that Paul offers verse 28 as an anchor.

"And we know..."

This is not wishful thinking. It is settled conviction. _Oidamen_ in Greek—we have seen, we have come to know, we are certain.²

---

### Peshat: The Plain Meaning

Let's break down the verse carefully.

**"And we know..."**

The "we" is believers—those Paul has been describing throughout Romans 8 as "in Christ Jesus" (v. 1), led by the Spirit (v. 14), adopted as children (v. 15). This is not a promise to everyone indiscriminately. It is a promise to those in covenant relationship with God.

**"...that for those who love God..."**

The promise is further qualified. It is for those who _love God_. This is not about perfect performance or flawless faith. But it is about orientation. Is your life aimed toward God? Is he the one you are seeking, however imperfectly?

**"...all things work together for good..."**

Here is the heart of the promise. Not "all things are good"—they are not. Not "all things feel good"—they often don't. But "all things work _together_" for good.

The image is of ingredients in a recipe. Some ingredients are bitter. Some are sour. Some, eaten alone, would be unpleasant or even harmful. But in the hands of a master chef, they are combined into something nourishing and delicious.³

**"...for those who are called according to his purpose."**

The final qualification. The promise rests on God's calling and God's purpose. This is not about our plans working out. It is about God's purposes being accomplished—and we are invited into that story.

## Section C: The Center — The Greek Deep-Dive

### συνεργέω (Synergeo) — "Work Together"

The Greek verb _synergeo_ (συνεργέω) is where we get the English word "synergy." It means to work together, to cooperate, to produce a combined effect.⁴

The prefix _syn_ means "together with." The root _ergon_ means "work" or "deed." The image is of multiple forces combining toward a unified result.

Here is the profound implication: the "things" in our lives do not work for good independently. They are _coordinated_. There is a hand weaving the threads, a master plan being executed, a purpose behind the apparent chaos.

This does not mean every individual event is good. A thread of black is still black. But in the tapestry, the black thread contributes to a pattern that would be incomplete without it.

Paul does not tell us we will always understand the pattern. He tells us there is one.

---

### ἀγαθός (Agathos) — "Good"

The Greek word for "good" here is _agathos_ (ἀγαθός), which has a specific meaning in biblical thought.

_Agathos_ is not merely pleasant or enjoyable. It refers to what is beneficial, advantageous, that which fulfills its proper purpose.⁵ A "good" knife is one that cuts well. A "good" shepherd is one who cares for the sheep effectively.

So when Paul says things work together for _agathos_, he is not promising pleasant outcomes. He is promising purposeful outcomes. The "good" in view is God's good—what he knows we need, which may differ significantly from what we want.

This becomes clearer when we read the next verse: "For those whom he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son" (Romans 8:29).

That is the "good." Conformity to Christ. Christlikeness. Becoming, through the refining fire of our circumstances, the people God designed us to be.

---

### πρόθεσις (Prothesis) — "Purpose"

The Greek word _prothesis_ (πρόθεσις) means a setting forth, a plan, a deliberate intention.⁶

It was used for the showbread in the temple—the "bread of the Presence" (_artoi tes protheseos_), the loaves set before the LORD in the holy place.⁷ The word carries connotations of something sacred, intentional, placed with deliberate purpose.

When Paul says believers are "called according to his _prothesis_," he is making a staggering claim: your life is not random. Your calling is not accidental. You have been placed, like the showbread, with deliberate intention in the presence of God.

This does not mean every detail of your life was scripted. But it means you are part of a larger design. Your story fits within _the_ Story. And the Author knows what he is doing.

---

### κλητός (Kletos) — "Called"

The word "called" is _kletos_ (κλητός), meaning summoned, invited, appointed.⁸

In the ancient world, a _kletos_ was someone invited to a feast or summoned to a purpose. The emphasis is not on the person's qualifications but on the one doing the calling. You are not _kletos_ because you are impressive. You are _kletos_ because God chose to invite you.

This is liberating. The promise of Romans 8:28 does not depend on your ability to love God perfectly or respond to him flawlessly. It depends on the one who called you. And he does not give up on what he starts.

"He who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ." (Philippians 1:6)

## Section B': The Christ Connection — The Remez

### The Ultimate "All Things"

There is no "all things working together for good" more dramatic than the cross.

Consider what happened on that Friday outside Jerusalem:

- The betrayal of a friend
- The abandonment of disciples
- The corruption of religious leaders
- The cowardice of a political governor
- The cruelty of Roman soldiers
- The mockery of passersby
- The physical agony of crucifixion
- The spiritual horror of bearing the world's sin
- The cry of dereliction: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"

Nothing about Good Friday felt good. Every circumstance was drenched in evil, injustice, and suffering. If there was ever a day that seemed to prove God was not in control, it was the day his Son hung dying on a criminal's cross.

And yet.

That day—that specific convergence of human wickedness and divine purpose—became the hinge of history. The worst thing that ever happened became the best thing that ever happened.⁹ The murder of the Son of God became the rescue of the world.

This is the ultimate demonstration of Romans 8:28. Not an abstract promise but a historical event. God took the worst that humans and demons could throw at him and wove it into salvation.

If he could do that with Calvary, he can do something with your Tuesday.

---

### Joseph's Testimony

The pattern appears throughout Scripture, but perhaps nowhere more explicitly than in the story of Joseph.

Sold into slavery by his brothers. Falsely accused by Potiphar's wife. Forgotten in prison for years. Every circumstance seemed designed to destroy him.

And yet, at the end of the story, Joseph looks at his brothers and says: "As for you, you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to bring it about that many people should be kept alive, as they are today" (Genesis 50:20).

The Hebrew verbs are the same: _chashav_ (חָשַׁב), to plan, to purpose, to intend.¹⁰ You _planned_ evil. God _planned_ good. Both intentions were real. Both were in operation. But God's purpose prevailed.

This is not God ignoring evil or pretending it doesn't exist. It is God overruling evil—taking it into his sovereign plan and redirecting it toward ends the evildoers never imagined.

---

### The Intercession of the Spirit

Romans 8:28 cannot be read apart from what immediately precedes it in verses 26-27:

> "Likewise the Spirit helps us in our weakness. For we do not know what to pray for as we ought, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us with groanings too deep for words. And he who searches hearts knows what is the mind of the Spirit, because the Spirit intercedes for the saints according to the will of God."

The "working together" of verse 28 is connected to the intercession of verse 27. The Spirit is not passive in our suffering. He is actively praying for us, advocating for us, aligning our circumstances with God's purposes even when we cannot pray coherent words.

This is crucial pastoral comfort. In your darkest moments—when you cannot think, cannot pray, cannot hope—the Spirit is at work. The divine Intercessor is bringing your situation before the Father and ensuring that even this will be woven into good.

You are not alone. You are not forgotten. You are being prayed for by God himself.

## Section A': The Return — Derash and Sod

### What This Means for Maria's Letter

Let's return to Maria and the letter she almost didn't open.

Eighteen years of shame. Eighteen years of wondering if she had made the worst mistake of her life. Eighteen years of a wound that never fully healed.

And then: a letter from a daughter who became a doctor. A letter that revealed not the ruin Maria had feared but a story of redemption she could never have scripted.

Does Romans 8:28 explain everything? No. There are still questions. Why did Maria have to carry that weight for so long? Why couldn't she have known sooner? Why did some choices lead to joy and others, for other women in similar situations, lead to ongoing pain?

The verse does not answer every question. But it makes a claim: even this was not wasted. Even this was being woven. Even this—the shame, the silence, the eighteen years of wondering—was part of a pattern that only God could see.

Maria's story is not typical. Many stories do not have such clear resolution this side of eternity. But Romans 8:28 is not promising tidy endings. It is promising that the endings we cannot yet see will, in God's economy, be good.

---

### The Necessary Qualifications

We must be honest about what this verse does not promise.

**It does not promise we will understand.** The "good" God is working toward may not be visible in this lifetime. We walk by faith, not by sight. Some threads will only make sense in glory.

**It does not promise the absence of pain.** Paul, who wrote these words, would be beaten, shipwrecked, imprisoned, and eventually executed. Knowing that all things work together for good did not spare him from suffering.

**It does not promise what we want.** The "good" is conformity to Christ (v. 29), not health, wealth, and comfortable circumstances. Sometimes the path to Christlikeness goes through the valley of the shadow of death.

**It does not promise a timeline.** The working together may take years, decades, generations. Joseph waited decades. Israel waited centuries. We may wait a lifetime.

But here is what it does promise: nothing is wasted. Not the pain. Not the loss. Not the failure. Not the betrayal. In the hands of a sovereign, loving God, everything is material for redemption.

---

### The Deeper Mystery (Sod)

There is a level of Romans 8:28 that goes beyond explanation into mystery.

How can free human choices and sovereign divine purposes both be real? How can genuine evil be overruled without being excused? How can God bring good from what is not good without becoming the author of evil?

These questions have occupied theologians for centuries, and they are not fully answerable this side of eternity. We are touching the deep things of God—the hidden counsels that Job could not understand, the ways that are higher than our ways.

But Scripture gives us this: God is both sovereign and good. He rules all things and intends only good for his children. These truths do not contradict each other, even when we cannot see how they fit together.

Faith is trusting the weaver when you cannot see the tapestry. It is believing in the recipe when you can only taste the bitter ingredients. It is holding onto Romans 8:28 in the hospital room, at the graveside, in the aftermath of betrayal—not because you understand, but because you trust the One who does.

---

### The Unshakable Conclusion

Paul follows Romans 8:28 with one of the most soaring passages in all of Scripture:

> "What then shall we say to these things? If God is for us, who can be against us? He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, how will he not also with him graciously give us all things?... Who shall separate us from the love of Christ?... For I am sure that neither death nor life, nor angels nor rulers, nor things present nor things to come, nor powers, nor height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of Christ Jesus our Lord." (Romans 8:31-39)

This is where the promise lands. Not in a neat explanation of suffering, but in an unshakable assurance of love.

Whatever happens—whatever you face, whatever you lose, whatever you cannot understand—you are loved. The God who gave his Son for you will not abandon you now. The things that are working together for good are orchestrated by One who has already proven his commitment at Calvary.

This is the ground beneath the promise. This is why we can trust that all things—even the things that feel like the end of everything—are being woven into a story that ends in glory.

## Breath Prayer

**Inhale:** "All things are working..."
**Exhale:** "...for my good."

_Repeat slowly for five breaths. Let the promise settle deeper than your circumstances._

## Reflection Questions

1. **What "thing" in your life right now feels impossible to redeem?** The failure you cannot undo, the loss you cannot recover, the choice you would give anything to take back. What would it mean to believe that even this is being woven into something good?

2. **How has something painful in your past already been used for good?** Can you trace, even partially, how God worked through a difficult season to produce something you now value? How might that memory inform your trust in current struggles?

3. **Where are you trying to see the whole tapestry?** What would it look like to release the need to understand and simply trust the Weaver? What specific circumstance might you need to surrender to God's "prothesis"—his deliberate, sacred purpose—today?
